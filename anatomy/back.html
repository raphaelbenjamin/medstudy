01
A 55-year-old man with severe coughing — TB of right lung extending to T6/T7, producing a "gibbus deformity." What condition is also confirmed radiographically?
Answer Choices
A. Lordosis
B. Kyphosis
C. Scoliosis
D. Spina bifida
E. Osteoarthritis
Explanation
Kyphosis is an abnormal increase in the thoracic curvature — the classic "hunchback." A gibbus deformity from TB is a focal angulated kyphosis at the lesion site. Lordosis is increased lumbar curvature ("swayback"). Scoliosis is lateral curvature with rotation. Spina bifida is a neural tube defect. Osteoarthritis is joint cartilage degeneration, not specific to thoracic curvature.
Active Recall
  • What is a gibbus deformity, and what disease classically causes it?
    A gibbus deformity is a focal, angular kyphosis at the site of vertebral destruction, classically caused by spinal tuberculosis (Pott's disease).
  • What's the difference between kyphosis, lordosis, and scoliosis?
    Kyphosis = increased thoracic forward curve; lordosis = increased lumbar/cervical backward curve; scoliosis = lateral curve with vertebral rotation.
  • Which spinal regions have primary vs secondary curvatures?
    Primary (kyphotic) curvatures are thoracic and sacral; secondary (lordotic) curvatures are cervical and lumbar.
02
A 68-year-old man with severe osteoporosis — crush fractures of L4 and L5. Which parts of the vertebrae are most likely fractured?
Answer Choices
A. Spinal process
B. Vertebral bodies
C. Transverse process
D. Superior articular process
E. Intervertebral disk
Explanation
Crush fractures involve compression of the entire vertebral body. Osteoporotic bone is weak and the body bears most axial load, making it vulnerable. Wedge fractures similarly affect vertebral bodies. Process fractures result from different mechanisms (oblique, transverse forces). Intervertebral disks herniate — they don't "crush fracture."
Active Recall
  • What is the difference between a crush fracture and a wedge fracture?
    A crush fracture compresses the entire vertebral body uniformly, while a wedge fracture collapses only the anterior portion of the body.
  • Why are vertebral bodies the most common site of osteoporotic fractures?
    Vertebral bodies bear most axial load and consist largely of cancellous bone, which becomes thin and fragile in osteoporosis.
  • What happens to overall height with vertebral body crush fractures?
    Overall height decreases progressively as each crushed vertebral body loses its normal height.
03
A 45-year-old man with back and lower limb pain — spinal stenosis syndrome. Which structure's hypertrophy is confirmed by MRI?
Answer Choices
A. Hypertrophy of supraspinous ligament
B. Hypertrophy of interspinous ligament
C. Hypertrophy of ligamentum flavum
D. Hypertrophy of anterior longitudinal ligament
E. Hypertrophy of nuchal ligament
Explanation
The ligamentum flavum connects laminae of adjacent vertebrae and forms the posterior wall of the vertebral canal. It's the only listed ligament in direct contact with the vertebral foramen, so its hypertrophy directly narrows the canal → spinal stenosis. The supraspinous and interspinous ligaments connect spinous processes (outside the canal). The anterior longitudinal ligament is on the front of vertebral bodies. The nuchal ligament is a thickened extension of the supraspinous ligament above C7.
Active Recall
  • What does the ligamentum flavum connect, and where is it located?
    The ligamentum flavum connects the laminae of adjacent vertebrae and forms the posterior wall of the vertebral canal.
  • Why does hypertrophy of the ligamentum flavum cause spinal stenosis but hypertrophy of other ligaments does not?
    It is the only spinal ligament located inside the vertebral canal in direct contact with the canal's posterior wall, so its hypertrophy directly narrows the canal.
  • What is the nuchal ligament, and at what level does it begin?
    The nuchal ligament is a thickened midline band in the posterior neck that is a rostral continuation of the supraspinous ligament, beginning at C7.
04
Car crash — fracture of medial border of right scapula, scapula retracted laterally. Which nerve is injured?
Answer Choices
A. Axillary
B. Long thoracic
C. Dorsal scapular
D. Greater occipital
E. Suprascapular
Explanation
The dorsal scapular nerve (C5) innervates the rhomboids (major and minor), which retract/adduct the scapula medially. Damage → the scapula drifts laterally. Don't confuse with long thoracic nerve injury — that causes "winging" of the scapula (serratus anterior paralysis), not lateral retraction. Axillary → deltoid/teres minor. Suprascapular → supraspinatus/infraspinatus.
Active Recall
  • What muscles does the dorsal scapular nerve supply?
    The dorsal scapular nerve (C5) supplies the rhomboid major, rhomboid minor, and levator scapulae.
  • How does dorsal scapular nerve palsy differ from long thoracic nerve palsy in presentation?
    Dorsal scapular palsy causes lateral drift of the scapula (loss of retraction); long thoracic palsy causes medial winging of the scapula (loss of protraction).
  • What is the root value of the dorsal scapular nerve?
    The dorsal scapular nerve arises from the C5 root.
05
Herpes zoster along C7 dermatome. In what structure has the virus proliferated?
Answer Choices
A. The sympathetic chain
B. The dorsal root ganglion of C7
C. The lateral horn of C7 spinal cord segment
D. The posterior cutaneous branch of dorsal primary ramus of C7
E. The ventral horn of C7 spinal cord segment
Explanation
Herpes zoster (shingles) remains latent in dorsal root ganglia of sensory nerves. When reactivated, it causes pain and vesicular eruptions along the dermatome. It's purely sensory — no motor involvement. The dorsal root ganglion is the only purely sensory structure listed. The ventral horn is motor, the lateral horn is autonomic, and peripheral nerve branches are downstream of the ganglion.
Active Recall
  • Where does herpes zoster virus remain latent?
    Herpes zoster virus remains latent in the dorsal root ganglia (DRG) of sensory spinal nerves after the primary chickenpox infection.
  • Why does shingles follow a dermatomal distribution?
    Reactivated virus travels anterograde along the sensory nerve to the skin it supplies, causing vesicles strictly within that dermatome.
  • Does herpes zoster have motor involvement?
    No — herpes zoster affects only sensory neurons (dorsal root ganglia); there is no motor involvement.
06
Disk herniation between L4 and L5. Which nerve is most likely affected?
Answer Choices
A. L1
B. L2
C. L3
D. L4
E. L5
Explanation
In the lumbar spine, a posterolateral disk herniation typically compresses the nerve that exits at the level BELOW (the "traversing" nerve root). Between L4/L5, the L4 nerve has already exited above the disk. The L5 nerve root descends posterior to the L4/L5 disk on its way to exit below L5 — so L5 gets compressed. Key rule: lumbar disk herniation affects the nerve numbered one below the disk level.
Active Recall
  • In a posterolateral lumbar disk herniation, which nerve root is compressed — the exiting or the traversing one?
    The traversing nerve root (the one passing behind the disk on its way to exit one level below) is compressed in a posterolateral herniation.
  • How does this differ from a far-lateral (foraminal) herniation?
    A far-lateral (foraminal) herniation compresses the exiting nerve root at that level rather than the traversing root.
  • What dermatome/myotome does L5 supply?
    L5 supplies the lateral leg, dorsum of foot, and great toe (motor: dorsiflexion and great toe extension).
07
A 3-year-old with meningitis needs a lumbar puncture. Most appropriate vertebral level?
Answer Choices
A. T12-L1
B. L1-2
C. L2-3
D. L4-5
E. L5-S1
Explanation
Lumbar puncture is done between L3/L4 or L4/L5. The spinal cord ends at L1-L2 in adults (can be L3 in newborns). The dural sac ends at S2. LP at L4-L5 is safely below the conus medullaris and within the lumbar cistern (subarachnoid space containing only cauda equina nerve roots floating in CSF).
Active Recall
  • At what vertebral level does the spinal cord end in adults vs. newborns?
    The spinal cord ends at L1–L2 in adults and as low as L3 in newborns.
  • What is the lumbar cistern?
    The lumbar cistern is the CSF-filled subarachnoid space below the conus medullaris (L1–L2) down to the end of the dural sac (S2), containing only cauda equina nerve roots.
  • Why is L4-L5 safe for LP?
    L4–L5 is below the conus medullaris, so only free-floating nerve roots (not cord) are present, greatly reducing the risk of cord injury.

What external landmark determines the L4 vertebral spine for lumbar puncture?
Answer Choices
A. The inferior angles of the scapulae
B. The iliac crests
C. The lowest pair of ribs bilaterally
D. The posterior superior iliac spines
E. The posterior inferior iliac spines
Explanation
A line drawn between the highest points of the iliac crests (the supracristal line / Tuffier's line) crosses the L4 spinous process or the L4/L5 interspace. Other landmarks: inferior scapular angles → T7; lowest ribs → T12; posterior superior iliac spines → S2.
Active Recall
  • What is Tuffier's line and what level does it mark?
    Tuffier's (supracristal) line connects the tops of both iliac crests and crosses the L4 spinous process or the L4/L5 interspace — the standard LP landmark.
  • What vertebral level corresponds to the inferior angle of the scapula?
    The inferior angle of the scapula corresponds to the T7 vertebral level.
  • What level do the posterior superior iliac spines correspond to?
    The posterior superior iliac spines (PSIS) correspond to the S2 vertebral level.
09
Whiplash injury — trauma to the ligament on the anterior surface of cervical vertebral bodies. Which ligament?
Answer Choices
A. Anterior longitudinal ligament
B. Ligamentum flavum
C. Nuchal ligament
D. Posterior longitudinal ligament
E. Transverse cervical ligament
Explanation
The anterior longitudinal ligament (ALL) runs along the anterior aspect of the vertebral bodies from C1 to sacrum. It prevents hyperextension. In rear-end collisions causing whiplash (hyperextension), the ALL is stretched/torn. The PLL is posterior to the vertebral bodies (inside the canal). Ligamentum flavum connects laminae posteriorly. Nuchal ligament is a posterior midline structure above C7.
Active Recall
  • What does the ALL prevent, and where does it run?
    The ALL runs along the anterior surface of vertebral bodies from the occiput to the sacrum and prevents hyperextension.
  • Which ligament prevents hyperflexion?
    The posterior longitudinal ligament (PLL) prevents hyperflexion.
  • What is the transverse cervical (cardinal) ligament actually associated with?
    The transverse cervical (cardinal) ligament is a pelvic ligament that supports the uterus — it is unrelated to the spine.
10
Compression of nerve elements at the L5/S1 intervertebral foramen. Which structure is responsible?
Answer Choices
A. Anulus fibrosus
B. Nucleus pulposus
C. Posterior longitudinal ligament
D. Anterior longitudinal ligament
E. Ligamentum flavum
Explanation
Disk herniation = the nucleus pulposus protrudes through a defect in the anulus fibrosus, typically posterolaterally into the spinal canal or intervertebral foramen. The nucleus pulposus is the gelatinous core that herniates. The anulus fibrosus is the outer ring that ruptures. The ligaments may be secondarily affected but aren't the compressing structure.
Active Recall
  • What are the two components of an intervertebral disk?
    An intervertebral disk has two components: the gelatinous nucleus pulposus (inner) and the fibrocartilaginous anulus fibrosus (outer ring).
  • Why do herniations typically occur posterolaterally?
    Posterolaterally, the anulus fibrosus is thinnest and the PLL is narrow, making it the path of least resistance for the herniating nucleus.
  • What is the role of the PLL in disk herniation?
    The PLL reinforces the posterior disk centrally, deflecting herniations away from the midline toward the weaker posterolateral region.
11
Car crash — weakness in medial rotation and adduction of the humerus. Which nerve is injured?
Answer Choices
A. Thoracodorsal
B. Axillary
C. Dorsal scapular
D. Spinal accessory
E. Radial
Explanation
The thoracodorsal nerve innervates the latissimus dorsi — a powerful adductor, medial rotator, and extensor of the humerus. Axillary → deltoid (abduction). Dorsal scapular → rhomboids (scapular retraction). Spinal accessory → trapezius/SCM. Radial → posterior compartment extensors. The other medial rotators (pectoralis major, subscapularis) are innervated by medial/lateral pectoral and subscapular nerves.
Active Recall
  • What muscle does the thoracodorsal nerve supply?
    The thoracodorsal nerve (C6–C8) supplies the latissimus dorsi.
  • Name the three major medial rotators of the humerus and their nerves.
    The three major medial rotators of the humerus are subscapularis (subscapular nerve), latissimus dorsi (thoracodorsal nerve), and pectoralis major (medial/lateral pectoral nerves).
12
Bilateral mastectomy — winging of both scapulae. Which nerves were damaged?
Answer Choices
A. Axillary
B. Spinal accessory
C. Long thoracic
D. Dorsal scapular
E. Thoracodorsal
Explanation
The long thoracic nerve (C5-C7) innervates the serratus anterior, which protracts the scapula and holds it against the thoracic wall. Damage → "winged scapula." This nerve runs superficially along the lateral thoracic wall and is vulnerable during mastectomy. The thoracodorsal nerve is also at risk during mastectomy but its injury causes weakness of latissimus dorsi, not winging.
Active Recall
  • What muscle does the long thoracic nerve supply?
    The long thoracic nerve (C5–C7) supplies the serratus anterior.
  • Why is the long thoracic nerve vulnerable during mastectomy?
    The long thoracic nerve runs superficially along the lateral thoracic wall without bony protection, making it vulnerable to stretch or direct injury during axillary dissection in mastectomy.
  • What is the clinical sign of serratus anterior paralysis?
    Serratus anterior paralysis causes medial winging of the scapula (the medial border lifts away from the thoracic wall, especially when pushing against a wall).
13
Shoulder dislocation — cannot initiate abduction. Which muscle is torn?
Answer Choices
A. Coracobrachialis
B. Long head of triceps
C. Pectoralis minor
D. Supraspinatus
E. Teres major
Explanation
Initiation of abduction (first 0–15°) is performed by the supraspinatus (suprascapular nerve, C5-C6). After that, the deltoid takes over (15–90°). Above horizontal, the trapezius and serratus anterior rotate the scapula to continue abduction. The supraspinatus is a rotator cuff muscle (SITS) and is commonly torn during shoulder dislocations.
Active Recall
  • What are the SITS muscles?
    The SITS muscles are: Supraspinatus, Infraspinatus, Teres minor, and Subscapularis (the four rotator cuff muscles).
  • Which muscle initiates abduction and which continues it?
    Supraspinatus initiates the first 0–15° of abduction; the deltoid then continues abduction from 15° to 90°.
  • What nerve supplies the supraspinatus?
    The supraspinatus is supplied by the suprascapular nerve (C5–C6).
14
1-year-old girl — skin dimple in lumbar region with tuft of hair. What is this common condition?
Answer Choices
A. Meningomyelocele
B. Meningocele
C. Spina bifida occulta
D. Spina bifida cystica
E. Rachischisis
Explanation
Spina bifida occulta is the mildest form — incomplete fusion of vertebral arches with no herniation of meninges or cord. Present in 10–25% of the population. Classic sign: tuft of hair and/or dimple over the lumbar region. Usually asymptomatic. The "cystica" forms (meningocele, meningomyelocele) involve protrusion of meninges ± cord. Rachischisis is the most severe form with open neural folds.
Active Recall
  • What is the spectrum of spina bifida from mildest to most severe?
    From mildest to most severe: spina bifida occulta (no herniation) → meningocele (meninges only) → meningomyelocele (meninges + neural tissue) → rachischisis (open spine).
  • What external signs suggest spina bifida occulta?
    Spina bifida occulta may show a tuft of hair (hypertrichosis) and/or a dimple or port-wine stain over the lower back.
  • What is the difference between meningocele and meningomyelocele?
    In meningocele only the meninges herniate (no neural tissue); in meningomyelocele both the meninges and spinal cord/nerve roots herniate through the defect.
15
Which nerve fibers carry sensation of a mosquito bite on the back lateral to T4 spinous process?
Answer Choices
A. Somatic afferent
B. Somatic efferent
C. Visceral afferent
D. Visceral efferent
E. Somatic efferent and visceral afferent
Explanation
Somatic afferents carry pain, pressure, touch, temperature, and proprioception from the body wall and limbs to the CNS. A mosquito bite on the skin = somatic sensation. "Afferent" = sensory (toward CNS). "Efferent" = motor (away from CNS). Visceral afferents carry info from internal organs. Visceral efferents supply smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
Active Recall
  • What is the difference between somatic and visceral afferents?
    Somatic afferents carry sharp, well-localized sensation from skin, muscles, and joints; visceral afferents carry dull, poorly-localized sensation from internal organs.
  • What types of tissue do visceral efferents innervate?
    Visceral efferents (autonomic motor fibers) innervate smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
16
Lumbar puncture for CSF — where must the needle tip be placed?
Answer Choices
A. In the epidural space
B. Between anterior and posterior longitudinal ligaments
C. Superficial to the ligamentum flavum
D. Between arachnoid mater and dura mater
E. In the subarachnoid space
Explanation
CSF circulates in the subarachnoid space (between arachnoid and pia mater). The epidural space contains fat and the internal vertebral venous plexus — no CSF. The subdural space (between dura and arachnoid) is only a potential space. Between the longitudinal ligaments is within/anterior to the vertebral body.
Active Recall
  • What are the three meninges from superficial to deep?
    From superficial to deep: dura mater → arachnoid mater → pia mater.
  • In which meningeal space is CSF found?
    CSF is found in the subarachnoid space (between the arachnoid and pia mater).
  • What is in the epidural space?
    The epidural space contains epidural fat and the internal vertebral venous plexus (Batson's plexus); there is no CSF here.
17
Cervical disk herniation — which ligament protects the spinal cord from direct compression?
Answer Choices
A. Supraspinous
B. Posterior longitudinal
C. Anterior longitudinal
D. Ligamentum flavum
E. Nuchal ligament
Explanation
The PLL runs along the posterior surface of the vertebral bodies inside the vertebral canal, directly between the disk and the spinal cord. It's the only ligament positioned to resist direct posterior herniation of the nucleus pulposus toward the cord. This is why most herniations go posterolaterally — the PLL is thicker in the midline.
Active Recall
  • Where does the PLL run relative to the vertebral bodies?
    The PLL runs along the posterior surface of the vertebral bodies inside the vertebral canal, directly between the disks and the spinal cord.
  • Why do most disk herniations occur posterolaterally rather than directly posteriorly?
    The PLL is wide and strong centrally but narrows laterally, leaving the posterolateral corner of the disk unprotected — the site of most herniations.

Spinal anesthesia at L4-L5 is safe because the spinal cord terminates at which level?
Answer Choices
A. T11 and T12
B. T12 and L1
C. L1 and L2
D. L2 and L3
E. L3 and L4
Explanation
The conus medullaris (terminal end of the spinal cord) is at the L1-L2 disk level in adults. In newborns it can be as low as L3. Below this level, only the cauda equina (nerve roots) and filum terminale are present in the lumbar cistern. The dural sac extends to S2.
Active Recall
  • What is the conus medullaris?
    The conus medullaris is the tapered inferior end of the spinal cord.
  • At what level does the conus medullaris end in adults vs. neonates?
    In adults the conus ends at L1–L2; in neonates it can end as low as L3.
  • Where does the dural sac end?
    The dural sac ends at S2.
19
Raynaud's disease — relief of hand symptoms requires division of which neural elements?
Answer Choices
A. Lower cervical and upper thoracic sympathetic fibers
B. Lower cervical and upper thoracic ventral roots
C. Lower cervical and upper thoracic dorsal roots
D. Lower cervical and upper thoracic spinal nerves
E. Bilateral spinal accessory nerves
Explanation
Raynaud's involves vasospasm from excessive sympathetic vasoconstriction. Sympathetic fibers control vascular smooth muscle tone. Sympathectomy (division of sympathetic chain ganglia) reduces sympathetic outflow to the hands. Cutting ventral roots would lose motor function; dorsal roots would lose sensation; spinal nerves would lose both. Only sympathetic division selectively addresses vasospasm.
Active Recall
  • What is the pathophysiology of Raynaud's disease?
    Raynaud's disease involves excessive sympathetic vasoconstriction causing episodic digital ischemia in response to cold or stress.
  • Why does sympathectomy help, and why can't you just cut spinal nerves?
    Sympathectomy reduces sympathetic tone to digital vessels; cutting spinal nerves would also abolish motor and general sensory function, which is unacceptable.
  • What spinal cord levels provide sympathetic outflow to the upper limb?
    Sympathetic fibers to the upper limb arise from the T2–T6 (or T1–T5) spinal cord levels.

Osteophytes at C2/C3 intervertebral foramen. Which muscle is most likely affected?

B. Serratus anterior
C. Supraspinatus

Active Recall
  • What are the root values of the phrenic nerve?
    The phrenic nerve arises from C3, C4, and C5.
  • What mnemonic helps remember phrenic nerve roots?
    Mnemonic: 'C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive.'
  • Which nerve exits at the C2/C3 foramen?
    The C3 nerve exits at the C2/C3 intervertebral foramen.
21
Laminectomy of two cervical vertebrae — which ligament is also removed?
Answer Choices
A. Anterior longitudinal
B. Denticulate
C. Ligamentum flavum
D. Nuchal
E. Cruciate
Explanation
The ligamentum flavum connects the laminae of adjacent vertebrae on their anterior surface (facing the canal). When the laminae are excised in a laminectomy, the ligamentum flavum is necessarily removed with them. ALL is on the anterior vertebral bodies. Denticulate ligaments are inside the dural sac (pia to dura). Nuchal ligament is posterior/midline. Cruciate ligament is anterior to the cord at C1/C2.
Active Recall
  • What exactly is removed in a laminectomy?
    In laminectomy the vertebral laminae (and the ligamentum flavum attached to their inner surface) are removed to decompress the spinal canal.
  • What does the ligamentum flavum connect?
    The ligamentum flavum connects the laminae of adjacent vertebrae along their anterior (canal-facing) surfaces.
  • Why is the ligamentum flavum inevitably removed during laminectomy?
    The ligamentum flavum attaches to the laminae; when the laminae are excised, the flavum is necessarily removed with them.

Caudal anesthetic via sacral hiatus — into which space is the anesthetic placed?
Answer Choices
A. Vertebral canal
B. Vertebral venous plexus
C. Epidural space
D. Subarachnoid space
E. Subdural space
Explanation
Caudal epidural anesthesia is administered through the sacral hiatus into the epidural space of the sacral canal. The dural sac ends at S2, so at the sacral hiatus level there is no subarachnoid space — only epidural fat and sacral nerve roots. The anesthetic bathes the sacral nerves in the epidural space.
Active Recall
  • Where does the dural sac end?
    The dural sac ends at S2.
  • What landmark is used to access the sacral canal?
    The sacral hiatus — the inferior opening of the sacral canal — is flanked by the sacral cornua and is used to access the caudal epidural space.
  • Why is the sacral hiatus below the subarachnoid space?
    The dural sac ends at S2, so the sacral hiatus (below S2) is entirely below the subarachnoid space — only epidural fat and sacral nerve roots are present there.
23
LP with hematopoietic cells in CSF — which ligament was penetrated incorrectly?
Answer Choices
A. Supraspinous
B. Denticulate
C. Anterior longitudinal
D. Posterior longitudinal
E. Nuchal ligament
Explanation
Hematopoietic cells come from bone marrow in the vertebral body. If the needle goes too deep anteriorly through the subarachnoid space, it would penetrate the PLL and enter the vertebral body. The PLL sits on the posterior aspect of vertebral bodies inside the canal. This indicates the needle was advanced too far — past the CSF, through the PLL, and into bone.
Active Recall
  • What does finding hematopoietic cells during LP indicate about needle placement?
    Finding hematopoietic cells indicates the needle has advanced too far anteriorly through the spinal canal and into the vertebral body (bone marrow).
  • What is the normal order of structures the LP needle passes through?
    Skin → subcutaneous tissue → supraspinous ligament → interspinous ligament → ligamentum flavum → epidural space → dura mater → subdural space → arachnoid mater → subarachnoid space.

Damage to transverse process of C3 with pulsating hematoma. Which artery?
Answer Choices
A. Anterior spinal artery
B. Vertebral artery
C. Ascending cervical artery
D. Deep cervical artery
E. Posterior spinal arteries
Explanation
The vertebral arteries ascend through the transverse foramina of C6–C1. Fracture of a cervical transverse process puts the vertebral artery at direct risk. Anterior and posterior spinal arteries run along the spinal cord inside the canal. The ascending cervical and deep cervical arteries are small branches that don't pass through the transverse foramina.
Active Recall
  • Through which cervical vertebrae do the vertebral arteries pass?
    The vertebral arteries ascend through the transverse foramina of C6 through C1.
  • What is the transverse foramen, and which vertebrae have them?
    The transverse foramen is a hole in the transverse process of cervical vertebrae (C1–C7) through which the vertebral arteries and veins pass (the artery is absent in C7's foramen).
25
79-year-old veteran — abnormally increased thoracic curvature from osteoporosis. Diagnosis?
Answer Choices
A. Scoliosis
B. Kyphosis
C. Spinal stenosis
D. Lordosis
E. Herniated disk
Explanation
Kyphosis = increased thoracic (primary) curvature. In elderly patients with osteoporosis, vertebral body compression fractures lead to progressive kyphotic deformity. Lordosis = increased lumbar/cervical (secondary) curvature. Scoliosis = lateral curvature. Spinal stenosis = narrowing of the vertebral canal.
Active Recall
  • Which curvatures are primary and which are secondary?
    Primary curvatures (kyphotic) are thoracic and sacral; secondary curvatures (lordotic) are cervical and lumbar.
  • How does osteoporosis lead to kyphosis?
    Osteoporosis causes vertebral body compression fractures that are greatest anteriorly, collapsing the front of the body and progressively increasing the thoracic kyphotic curve.

Neck injury — shoulder drooping, difficulty elevating shoulder. Which nerve?
Answer Choices
A. Thoracodorsal nerve
B. Spinal accessory nerve
C. Dorsal scapular nerve
D. Greater occipital nerve
E. Axillary nerve
Explanation
The spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) innervates the trapezius and SCM. Trapezius elevates the shoulder and rotates the scapula. CN XI injury → drooping shoulder and inability to shrug. Test: shrug shoulders against resistance (trapezius) and turn head against resistance (SCM).
Active Recall
  • What two muscles does CN XI innervate?
    CN XI (spinal accessory nerve) innervates the trapezius and sternocleidomastoid (SCM).
  • How do you clinically test CN XI?
    Test CN XI by asking the patient to shrug their shoulders against resistance (trapezius) and turn their head against resistance (SCM).
  • Why is CN XI vulnerable in the posterior triangle of the neck?
    CN XI traverses the posterior triangle of the neck superficially without bony protection, making it easily damaged by trauma, lymph node biopsy, or dissection.

Head-on collision — dens compresses spinal cord causing quadriplegia. Which ligament tore?
Answer Choices
A. Anterior longitudinal ligament
B. Transverse ligament of the atlas
C. Ligamentum flavum
D. Supraspinous ligament
