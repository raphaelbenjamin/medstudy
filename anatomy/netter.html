I. Thorax Heart · Lungs · Mediastinum

Coronary arteries, cardiac veins, valves, conducting system, lungs, great vessels

Heart — Coronary Arteries & Cardiac Veins
Coronary arteries — territories, SA node supply & clinical
▸
Core Anatomy

Two coronary arteries arise from the aortic sinuses just above the aortic valve:

Left coronary artery (LCA): Short left main → splits into (1) Left anterior descending (LAD) — supplies anterior wall of LV, anterior 2/3 of interventricular septum, apex; and (2) Left circumflex (LCx) — supplies lateral and posterior wall of LV.

Right coronary artery (RCA): Travels in the right AV groove → gives off acute marginal branches → continues as posterior descending artery (PDA) in ~85% of people (right-dominant). Supplies: right atrium, right ventricle, diaphragmatic (inferior) surface of the heart, posterior 1/3 of the interventricular septum, SA node (~60%), AV node.

Exam Answers
  • MI of diaphragmatic surface → RCA occluded (the RCA/PDA supplies the inferior/diaphragmatic surface)
  • Infarction of posterior 1/3 of IV septum → Posterior interventricular branch of the RCA (= PDA)
  • SA node blood supply in most people → RCA (~60%), circumflex in ~40%
  • SA node location → between the SVC and the superior border of the crista terminalis

Cardiac veins — which vein runs with the RCA?
▸
Core Anatomy

Cardiac veins parallel the coronary arteries:

  • Great cardiac vein — runs with the LAD in the anterior IV groove, then with LCx → drains into the coronary sinus
  • Middle cardiac vein — runs with the PDA/posterior IV branch of RCA in the posterior IV groove
  • Small cardiac vein — runs with the RCA (right coronary margin), drains into coronary sinus
  • Anterior cardiac veins — drain directly into the right atrium (do NOT drain into coronary sinus)

Exam answer: Surgeon suturing the distal RCA accidentally punctures the adjacent vein → small cardiac vein (runs alongside the RCA). However, some sources say middle cardiac vein since the "distal" RCA is near where it becomes the PDA in the posterior IV groove. This is commonly tested — the middle cardiac vein accompanies the PDA at the distal end of the RCA territory.

Heart — Chambers, Valves & Conducting System
Chambers, valves, S1 sound, moderator band & key features
▸
Key Facts

Aortic valve cusps: Right, Left, and Posterior (NOT anterior). Mnemonic: the aortic valve has R, L, P cusps. The pulmonary valve has R, L, and Anterior.

S1 heart sound: Produced by closure of the AV valves = mitral + tricuspid (beginning of systole). S2 = closure of aortic + pulmonary (semilunar valves).

Pectinate muscles: Ridge-like muscles in the atrial walls. They extend anterolaterally from the crista terminalis (terminal crest) in the RA. The septomarginal trabecula (moderator band) is in the RIGHT ventricle.

Moderator band (septomarginal trabecula): Carries part of the right bundle branch to the anterior papillary muscle of the right ventricle. It carries the LEFT branch? NO — "The septomarginal trabecula carries part of the LEFT branch" is WRONG. It carries the RIGHT bundle branch.

Apex of the heart: Formed by the left ventricle, lies in the left 5th intercostal space, midclavicular line.

Epicardium = visceral layer of serous pericardium. Correct.

Left atrium compression: A dilated left atrium presses on the esophagus (directly posterior) → dysphagia.

Exam Answers
  • "Septomarginal trabecula carries part of the LEFT branch" → WRONG (it carries the RIGHT bundle branch). This is the exception.
  • S1 = mitral + tricuspid valve closure
  • Aortic valve cusps = right, left, posterior
  • Dilated left atrium compresses → esophagus
  • Moderator band → anterior papillary muscle of RV

Fetal circulation, PDA & foramen ovale
▸
Core Concept

Three fetal shunts:

  • Ductus venosus: Umbilical vein → bypasses liver → IVC (becomes ligamentum venosum)
  • Foramen ovale: RA → LA (bypasses lungs). Blood is directed through it by the valve of the IVC (Eustachian valve), which directs oxygenated blood from the IVC toward the foramen ovale. Becomes fossa ovalis.
  • Ductus arteriosus: Pulmonary artery → aorta (bypasses lungs). Becomes ligamentum arteriosum.

Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA): If contrast injected into aortic arch immediately appears in both pulmonary arteries → PDA (aorta communicates with pulmonary trunk).

Foramen ovale blood shunt: Directed by the Eustachian valve (valve of the IVC), which in fetal life guides oxygenated blood from the IVC across the foramen ovale into the LA.

Esophageal varices & portosystemic anastomoses
▸
Core Concept

In portal hypertension, blood from the portal system finds alternative routes to reach the systemic venous circulation. Esophageal varices are the most dangerous portosystemic anastomosis.

Esophageal varices: Left gastric vein (portal) ↔ submucosal esophageal veins → azygos system (systemic). When portal pressure rises, these submucosal veins engorge → varices → risk of fatal hemorrhage.

Lungs, Airways & Thoracic Wall
Lungs, bronchi, pleura, ribs, diaphragm & sternal angle
▸
High-Yield Facts (All Exam-Tested)

Azygos vein: Arches over the right main bronchus, draining into the SVC.

Left main bronchus: Passes inferior to the arch of the aorta, anterior to the esophagus, and anterior to the thoracic (descending) aorta. The left bronchus is longer and more horizontal than the right.

Cervical pleura: Covers the apices of the lungs (the part of the lung that projects above the 1st rib into the root of the neck).

Middle lobe segments (right lung): Lateral and medial segments. (The middle lobe only exists on the right.)

Oblique fissure: Projects anteriorly at the 6th rib/6th costal cartilage (some sources: 4th–5th intercostal space laterally, ending at the 6th costochondral junction anteriorly).

Rib 8 articulation: A typical rib head articulates with: (1) inferior costal facet of the vertebra ABOVE, (2) superior costal facet of its own vertebra, (3) transverse process of its own vertebra. So Rib 8 → inferior costal facet of T7, superior costal facet of T8, transverse process of T8.

Sternal angle (of Louis): Marks the level of the 2nd rib articulating with the sternum. Also marks: tracheal bifurcation (carina), beginning/end of aortic arch, azygos vein arch, T4-T5 level.

Carina level: T4-T5 (at the sternal angle).

Diaphragm innervation: Phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5) — "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive." Injury to the phrenic nerve in the posterior triangle of the neck → diaphragmatic paralysis. Phrenic nerve roots = C3-C5.

Breast lymphatic drainage
▸
Core Concept

Lymph from the lateral quadrants of the breast drains first into the pectoral (anterior) axillary lymph nodes. ~75% of all breast lymph drains to the axillary nodes. The medial quadrants drain to the parasternal (internal thoracic) nodes.

II. Abdomen GI · Peritoneum · Abdominal Wall

Peritoneum, Omental Foramen & Clinical Scenarios
Omental foramen, lesser sac, appendicitis & gastric ulcer
▸
Omental Foramen (of Winslow/Epiploic Foramen)

The only natural communication between the greater and lesser peritoneal sacs. Boundaries:

  • Anterior: Hepatoduodenal ligament (containing portal triad: portal vein, hepatic artery proper, bile duct)
  • Posterior: IVC (inferior vena cava)
  • Superior: Caudate lobe of liver
  • Inferior: 1st part of duodenum

Mnemonic for order: Anterior = hepatoduodenal ligament, Posterior = IVC, Superior = liver, Inferior = duodenum.

Clinical Scenarios

Posterior gastric ulcer perforation: Stomach's posterior wall faces the omental bursa (lesser sac). Perforation → contents spill into the lesser sac → peritonitis develops initially in the omental bursa.

Appendicitis (psoas sign): Pain around umbilicus, fever, positive psoas test (flexion of hip against resistance causes RLQ pain) → appendix is inflamed (retrocecal appendix lies on psoas muscle).

Hepatocolic ligament: Part of the greater omentum system (connects liver to hepatic flexure of colon).

Biliary system, pancreas, spleen & portal triad
▸
Key Facts

Pancreatic head tumor + jaundice: The head of the pancreas wraps around the bile duct (common bile duct). A tumor here obstructs the bile duct → obstructive jaundice. (Not the cystic duct, not the hepatic ducts — specifically the bile duct as it passes through/behind the head.)

Hepatopancreatic ampulla (of Vater): Formed by the union of the main pancreatic duct + bile duct. Opens into the 2nd part of the duodenum at the major duodenal papilla.

Portal triad (in hepatoduodenal ligament): Portal vein + hepatic artery proper + bile duct. During liver transplantation, clamping the portal triad includes the hepatic artery proper.

Spleen: Located in the left hypochondriac region (NOT right). Supplied by the splenic artery (branch of the celiac trunk, NOT SMA). Does NOT normally extend below the costal margin (if palpable, it's enlarged). Attached to the stomach by the gastrosplenic ligament, to the kidney by the splenorenal (lienorenal) ligament.

Renal vein occlusion: The LEFT renal vein crosses anterior to the aorta but POSTERIOR to the superior mesenteric artery (SMA). An SMA aneurysm can compress and occlude the left renal vein ("nutcracker syndrome").

Stab wound at T10 midaxillary line on the LEFT: → Spleen (ribs 9–11, left side).

Inguinal Region & Hernias
Direct vs indirect inguinal vs femoral hernias & Hesselbach triangle
▸
Core Anatomy

Indirect inguinal hernia: Goes through the deep (internal) inguinal ring, LATERAL to the inferior epigastric vessels, through the inguinal canal, may exit through the superficial ring into the scrotum. Most common hernia in BOTH men and women overall.

Direct inguinal hernia: Pushes through the posterior wall of the inguinal canal (Hesselbach triangle), MEDIAL to the inferior epigastric vessels. Does NOT pass through the deep ring.

Hesselbach triangle borders: (1) Inferior epigastric artery (lateral), (2) Rectus abdominis (medial), (3) Inguinal ligament (inferior).

Femoral hernia: Passes through the femoral ring, inferior to the inguinal ligament, medial to the femoral vein. More common in women due to wider pelvis. Highest risk of incarceration/strangulation.

Exam answers:

  • Loop of intestine medial to inferior epigastric vessels → direct inguinal hernia
  • Mass inferior to inguinal ligament, medial to femoral vein → femoral hernia
  • Most common hernia in women → indirect inguinal (most common hernia in all people, though femoral is more common in women than in men)

III. Pelvis & Perineum Uterus · Bladder · Testis

Pelvic Anatomy & Clinical
Pouch of Douglas, uterine artery/ureter, testicular drainage & uterine position
▸
Key Facts

Rectouterine pouch (of Douglas): The lowest point of the peritoneal cavity in females. Located between the uterus and rectum. Accessed by needle through the posterior vaginal fornix (culdocentesis) to drain ascites. Also: if an instrument perforates the posterior wall of the uterus through the cervical os → enters the rectouterine pouch.

Uterine artery and ureter — "water under the bridge": The ureter passes posterior and inferior to the uterine artery at the level of the cervix. The ureter is the "water" passing "under the bridge" (uterine artery). This is critical during hysterectomy — surgeons must ligate the uterine artery carefully to avoid cutting the ureter.

Normal uterine position: Anteverted (angle between cervix and vagina tilts forward) and anteflexed (body of uterus bends forward on the cervix).

Testicular lymphatic drainage: Testes drain to para-aortic (lumbar) lymph nodes — NOT inguinal nodes. This is because the testes developed retroperitoneally and descended, taking their lymphatic drainage with them. The scrotum (skin) drains to superficial inguinal nodes, but the testis itself → para-aortic.

IV. Head & Neck Cranial Nerves · Larynx · Teeth

Cranial Nerves — Clinical Scenarios
Vagus (CN X) & hypoglossal (CN XII) — uvula & tongue deviation
▸
Core Concept

Vagus nerve (CN X) lesion → uvula deviates AWAY from the lesion (toward the normal side):

  • The vagus innervates the muscles of the soft palate (via pharyngeal plexus) and larynx
  • If the LEFT vagus is damaged → the left side of the soft palate is paralyzed → the intact right side pulls the uvula to the RIGHT (away from the lesion)
  • Also causes: sagging of the soft palate on the affected side, hoarseness (recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement)

Exam: Uvula deviates to the right → LEFT vagus nerve lesion.

Hypoglossal nerve (CN XII) lesion → tongue deviates TOWARD the affected side:

  • The hypoglossal nerve innervates all intrinsic and most extrinsic tongue muscles (except palatoglossus = vagus)
  • If the RIGHT hypoglossal is damaged → right genioglossus is paralyzed → the intact LEFT genioglossus pushes the tongue to the RIGHT (toward the lesion)
  • Mnemonic: "The tongue licks the wound" — deviates toward the damaged side

Exam: Tongue deviates to the right → RIGHT hypoglossal nerve lesion (right hypoglossal nucleus).

Tongue taste vs sensation, inferior alveolar nerve & teeth
▸
Core Concept

Anterior 2/3 of tongue:

  • General sensation (touch, pain, temperature): Lingual nerve (branch of V3, mandibular division of trigeminal)
  • Taste: Chorda tympani (branch of CN VII, facial nerve) — hitchhikes with the lingual nerve

Posterior 1/3 of tongue: Both taste AND general sensation → glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX)

Exam scenario: "Taste in anterior 2/3 is lost but general sensation remains" → the chorda tympani is damaged (within the facial canal, before it joins the lingual nerve). The lingual nerve itself is intact (sensation preserved).

Inferior alveolar nerve: Branch of V3 (mandibular). Enters the mandibular foramen → runs in the mandibular canal → supplies all mandibular teeth. To treat mandibular molar caries → anesthetize the inferior alveolar nerve (inferior alveolar nerve block).

Tooth surfaces (exam trap): The mesial surface of a posterior tooth faces ANTERIORLY (toward the midline of the dental arch), NOT posteriorly. "Mesial surface of posterior teeth faces posteriorly" is WRONG.

Larynx & Neck
Laryngeal muscles, rima glottidis, carotid sheath & neck anatomy
▸
Key Facts

Primary tensor of the vocal folds: Cricothyroid muscle — the ONLY intrinsic laryngeal muscle innervated by the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve (all others = recurrent laryngeal nerve). It elongates and tenses the vocal folds.

Only ABductor of the vocal folds: Posterior cricoarytenoid — vital for opening the airway. All other intrinsic muscles are adductors or tensors. In bilateral vocal cord paralysis, this is the muscle that fails → cords in midline → airway compromise.

Rima glottidis: The opening between the vocal folds. This is the narrowest part of the adult larynx. (In children, the narrowest part is the subglottic area at the cricoid ring.)

Conus elasticus (cricovocal membrane): The upper free edge forms the vocal ligament (the true vocal fold edge). Attaches inferiorly to the cricoid cartilage.

Carotid sheath contents: Carotid arteries medially, internal jugular vein laterally, vagus nerve posteriorly (between them).

Lower lip carcinoma lymph drainage: → submental lymph nodes first.

Orbital floor blowout fracture: The thin orbital floor breaks → contents herniate into the maxillary sinus (which lies directly below the orbit).

V. Upper Limb Brachial Plexus · Shoulder · Hand

Nerves, Fractures & Clinical Scenarios
All upper limb nerve injuries, fractures & clinical tests
▸
Nerve Injuries — All Exam Scenarios

Fractured pisiform + hamate → ulnar nerve injury. The ulnar nerve passes through Guyon's canal between the pisiform and hook of hamate. Fractures here damage it.

Weakness of abduction 0–15° → suprascapular nerve. The supraspinatus (innervated by suprascapular nerve) initiates abduction 0–15°. Deltoid (axillary nerve) takes over 15–90°. The question specifies 0–15° specifically = suprascapular.

Surgical neck of humerus fracture → axillary nerve. The axillary nerve wraps around the surgical neck through the quadrangular space. Injury → loss of deltoid function (abduction 15–90°) + loss of sensation over the regimental badge area (lateral shoulder).

Scapula fracture — which muscle is LEAST affected? → Brachialis. The brachialis originates from the anterior humerus shaft, NOT the scapula. All others listed (biceps, deltoid, pectoralis minor, latissimus dorsi) have scapular attachments.

Scaphoid fracture: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH) + tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox + normal initial X-ray → suspect scaphoid fracture. Risk of avascular necrosis due to retrograde blood supply. Most commonly fractured carpal bone.

Winging scapula (pain on resisted forward pushing, NOT abduction): → Long thoracic nerve (supplies serratus anterior). Serratus anterior holds the scapula against the thoracic wall. Loss → winging, especially with forward pushing (wall push-up). If winging worsened with arm abduction only → think spinal accessory nerve (trapezius).

Pancoast tumor (superior thoracic aperture) + pain down medial arm: → Inferior trunk (C8-T1) of the brachial plexus. The inferior trunk runs over the first rib and can be compressed by an apical lung tumor.

Nursemaid's elbow: Sudden pull on extended pronated forearm in a child → subluxation of the radial head from the annular ligament.

Interosseous membrane force transmission: Transmits force from the radius → ulna. When you fall on your hand, the force goes through the wrist to the radius, then across the interosseous membrane to the ulna, then to the humerus.

Can't lift arm above shoulder but abduction intact: This describes loss of trapezius function (upper fibers elevate past 90°) or possibly deltoid weakness. If abduction to 90° is possible but not beyond → think spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) — trapezius needed for full overhead elevation.

VI. Lower Limb Hip · Knee · Nerves

Hip, Knee & Lower Limb Nerves
All lower limb nerve injuries, hip, knee & clinical tests
▸
Nerve Injuries — All Exam Scenarios

Positive Trendelenburg sign (standing on RIGHT leg, pelvis drops on LEFT): → RIGHT superior gluteal nerve is damaged. The superior gluteal nerve innervates gluteus medius and minimus (hip abductors). When you stand on one leg, these muscles on the STANDING side hold the pelvis level. If they're weak, the pelvis drops on the opposite side. The damaged nerve is on the SAME side as the standing leg.

Loss of hip adduction after pelvic surgery: → Obturator nerve (L2-L4). The obturator nerve innervates the adductor muscles of the thigh (adductor longus, brevis, magnus, gracilis, obturator externus).

Foot drop (can't dorsiflex): → Common peroneal (fibular) nerve — wraps around the neck of the fibula (very vulnerable). The deep peroneal branch specifically controls dorsiflexion; the common peroneal is what's injured at the fibular neck. Proximal fibula fracture → common peroneal/fibular nerve damage → foot drop.

Anterior drawer test positive (tibia slides anteriorly on femur): → Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) tear. Posterior drawer (tibia slides posteriorly) → PCL tear.

Patellar ligament insertion: → Tibial tuberosity. The quadriceps tendon → patella → patellar ligament → tibial tuberosity.

Sciatic nerve injection: Safe site = upper lateral (superolateral) quadrant of the buttock. This avoids the sciatic nerve which runs through the lower/medial part.

Posterior hip dislocation: Most common type. The ligamentum teres (round ligament of the head of the femur) is most likely torn first — it's the weakest ligament inside the joint. The iliofemoral (Y ligament) is the strongest.

Femoral triangle: Borders: inguinal ligament (superior), sartorius (lateral), adductor longus (medial). Floor: iliopsoas (laterally) + pectineus and adductor longus (medially).

L3-L4 disc herniation → compresses L4 nerve root. In the lumbar spine, a disc herniates posterolaterally and compresses the nerve ROOT BELOW (L3-L4 disc → L4 nerve). This differs from cervical spine where the nerve root above is affected.

VII. Back & Spinal Cord Vertebrae · Spinal Nerves · LP

Spinal Cord, Nerve Roots & Lumbar Puncture
Conus medullaris, lumbar puncture, IVF nerves & dermatomes
▸
Key Facts

Conus medullaris: The tapering end of the spinal cord. In adults, located at L1-L2 vertebral level. (In newborns it's at L3.) Below this is only the cauda equina (nerve roots, not spinal cord).

Lumbar puncture at L3-L4: The needle passes through (superficial to deep): skin → subcutaneous tissue → supraspinous ligament → interspinous ligament → ligamentum flavum → epidural (extradural) space → dura mater → arachnoid mater → subarachnoid space (CSF). After the ligamentum flavum, the next structure is the epidural space, then dura.

C7-T1 IVF narrowing → C8 nerve compressed. In the cervical spine, there are 8 cervical nerves but only 7 cervical vertebrae. C1-C7 nerves exit ABOVE their respective vertebrae. C8 exits between C7 and T1. So narrowing of the C7-T1 foramen → C8 nerve root.

T10 dermatome = umbilicus. Loss of vibratory sense below the umbilicus with preserved pain → dorsal column lesion at T10. Vibratory sense and proprioception travel in the dorsal columns (posterior funiculus). Pain/temperature travel in the spinothalamic tract (anterolateral).
