Bacteria — Classification / Structure / Gram Stain
Gram staining technique
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: The Gram stain is the most fundamental differential staining technique in microbiology. Developed by Hans Christian Gram in 1884, it classifies bacteria into two major groups — Gram-positive (purple) and Gram-negative (pink/red) — based on differences in cell wall structure. The procedure involves four steps: application of crystal violet (primary stain), Gram's iodine (mordant), alcohol/acetone (decolorizer), and safranin (counterstain).

Why it matters: It is the first step in bacterial identification and directly guides empirical antibiotic therapy. A Gram stain result from a clinical specimen (blood, CSF, sputum) can give critical information within minutes, long before cultures are ready.

Key characteristics: Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer (20–80 nm) that retains crystal violet–iodine complexes after decolorization. Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer (1–3 nm) sandwiched between an inner and outer membrane; the outer membrane contains lipopolysaccharide (LPS). They lose the crystal violet during decolorization and pick up the pink safranin counterstain.

Related systems: Foundational for all of clinical microbiology, infectious disease diagnosis, and antibiotic selection.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Mechanism (step by step): (1) Crystal violet binds to the bacterial cell wall. (2) Gram's iodine forms a crystal violet–iodine (CV-I) complex, which is large and becomes trapped. (3) Decolorizer (alcohol or acetone) dehydrates the thick Gram-positive wall, shrinking the pores and locking in the CV-I complex; in Gram-negatives, it dissolves the outer membrane and washes the CV-I complex out through the thin peptidoglycan. (4) Safranin counterstains the now-colorless Gram-negative cells pink/red.

Key structural differences: Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan, teichoic acids, no outer membrane, no LPS. Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan, outer membrane with LPS (endotoxin), periplasmic space, porins.

Common pitfalls: Over-decolorization can make Gram-positives appear Gram-negative. Under-decolorization can make Gram-negatives appear Gram-positive. Old cultures may stain variably.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: The order of reagents (crystal violet → iodine → decolorizer → safranin), the role of each step, and why Gram-positives retain the stain (thick peptidoglycan traps the CV-I complex). Also tested: which organisms are Gram-positive vs Gram-negative, and what clinical information a Gram stain provides.

Buzzwords: "Thick peptidoglycan," "crystal violet–iodine complex," "lipopolysaccharide," "outer membrane."

Differential: Organisms that stain poorly or atypically — Mycobacteria (acid-fast, not Gram-stainable), Mycoplasma (no cell wall), Chlamydia (intracellular, too small).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Four steps in order: crystal violet, iodine (mordant), decolorizer (alcohol/acetone), safranin (counterstain)
  • Gram-positive = purple/blue; Gram-negative = pink/red
  • The decolorization step is the critical/differential step
  • Gram-positive: thick peptidoglycan (20–80 nm), teichoic acids, no outer membrane
  • Gram-negative: thin peptidoglycan, outer membrane with LPS, periplasmic space, porins
  • LPS = endotoxin, a major virulence factor of Gram-negatives
  • Organisms that don't Gram stain well: Mycobacteria, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Treponema
  • Gram stain gives rapid preliminary ID from clinical specimens — guides empirical therapy

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

Think of the Gram stain as a "wall test" — thick wall = keeps the dye in, thin wall = dye washes out. The mordant (iodine) makes the dye complex bigger so it gets trapped more effectively. The decolorizer is the make-or-break step: too much and everything looks Gram-negative, too little and everything looks Gram-positive. A good mnemonic for reagent order: Come In, De Stain (Crystal violet, Iodine, Decolorizer, Safranin).

Ziehl-Neelsen staining
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: The Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) stain is an acid-fast staining technique used to identify Mycobacteria (especially M. tuberculosis and M. leprae) and other acid-fast organisms such as Nocardia (partially/weakly acid-fast). These organisms have cell walls rich in mycolic acids — long-chain fatty acids that make the wall waxy, hydrophobic, and resistant to conventional Gram staining.

Why it matters: Tuberculosis remains one of the leading infectious causes of death worldwide. The ZN stain is a rapid, low-cost method for detecting acid-fast bacilli (AFB) in clinical specimens like sputum, and is essential for TB diagnosis particularly in resource-limited settings.

Key characteristics: Uses carbol fuchsin (red primary stain), heat to drive the stain into the waxy wall, acid-alcohol as decolorizer, and methylene blue as counterstain. Acid-fast organisms appear bright red against a blue background.

Related systems: Pulmonary medicine (TB), dermatology (leprosy), infectious diseases. Also relevant: modified acid-fast stains for Cryptosporidium and Cyclospora (parasitology).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Mechanism: (1) Carbol fuchsin is applied and heated — heat opens the waxy mycolic acid layer to allow dye penetration. (2) Once inside, the dye binds tightly to the mycolic acids. (3) Acid-alcohol decolorizer is applied — organisms with mycolic acids resist decolorization (acid-fast), while other bacteria lose the dye. (4) Methylene blue counterstain colors non-acid-fast organisms blue.

Why Gram stain fails: Mycolic acids are so hydrophobic that crystal violet cannot penetrate the cell wall effectively. The ZN stain uses heat and a phenol-based stain to overcome this barrier.

Variants: Kinyoun stain (cold acid-fast stain) uses a higher concentration of carbol fuchsin without heat. Auramine-rhodamine is a fluorescent acid-fast stain that is more sensitive but requires a fluorescence microscope.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Why Mycobacteria are acid-fast (mycolic acids), the principle of the ZN stain, appearance of AFB (red rods on blue background). Also tested: which organisms are acid-fast (Mycobacterium, Nocardia — weakly).

Buzzwords: "Acid-fast bacilli," "mycolic acids," "carbol fuchsin," "red rods on blue background," "waxy cell wall."

Differential: Partially acid-fast organisms (Nocardia) vs fully acid-fast (Mycobacterium). Modified ZN for parasites (Cryptosporidium).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Identifies acid-fast organisms — primarily Mycobacteria (TB, leprosy)
  • Acid-fastness is due to mycolic acids in the cell wall
  • Steps: carbol fuchsin + heat → acid-alcohol decolorizer → methylene blue counterstain
  • Acid-fast = red/pink; non-acid-fast = blue
  • Heat is required to force the stain through the waxy mycolic acid layer
  • Nocardia is partially/weakly acid-fast
  • Mycobacteria do NOT Gram stain reliably (sometimes called "Gram-ghosts")
  • Modified ZN (no heat, milder decolorizer) can detect Cryptosporidium oocysts
5 · Active Recall Prompts
  • What cell wall component makes Mycobacteria acid-fast?
  • What is the primary stain used in the Ziehl-Neelsen technique?
  • Why is heat applied during the ZN stain?
  • What color do acid-fast bacilli appear under microscopy?
  • Name one organism that is weakly/partially acid-fast.
  • How does the Kinyoun stain differ from the classical ZN stain?
  • Why can't Mycobacteria be reliably identified by Gram stain?
  • What parasite can be detected using a modified acid-fast stain?
6 · Additional Insights / Tips

Remember: mycolic acids = waxy = waterproof = needs heat to get the dye in, and once in, the acid can't get it back out. That's the whole principle of "acid-fast." Think of it like melting wax to push dye in, then the wax re-solidifies and locks it in place. TB is the classic board scenario — patient with chronic cough, night sweats, weight loss, and the ZN stain shows red bacilli on a blue background.

Staphylococci
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Staphylococci are Gram-positive cocci that arrange in grape-like clusters. They are catalase-positive, which distinguishes them from Streptococci (catalase-negative). The genus includes important human pathogens: S. aureus (coagulase-positive), S. epidermidis and S. saprophyticus (both coagulase-negative).

Why it matters: S. aureus is one of the most common and clinically significant human pathogens, causing skin infections, bacteremia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis, pneumonia, and toxic shock syndrome. Coagulase-negative staphylococci are leading causes of prosthetic device and catheter-related infections.

Key characteristics: Facultative anaerobes, salt-tolerant (grow on mannitol salt agar), normal flora of skin and nares. Classified by coagulase test: coagulase-positive (S. aureus) vs coagulase-negative (S. epidermidis, S. saprophyticus).

Related systems: Skin/soft tissue, blood, bone, heart valves, urinary tract (S. saprophyticus), prosthetic devices (S. epidermidis).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Key distinguishing tests: Catalase-positive (differentiates from Streptococci). Coagulase test separates S. aureus (positive) from CoNS. S. aureus ferments mannitol (yellow colonies on mannitol salt agar). S. aureus is also protein A positive (binds IgG Fc region, preventing opsonization). S. saprophyticus is novobiocin-resistant (vs S. epidermidis which is novobiocin-sensitive).

Virulence factors of S. aureus: Protein A, coagulase, catalase, hemolysins (alpha-toxin), Panton-Valentine leukocidin (PVL), enterotoxins (food poisoning), TSST-1 (toxic shock), exfoliative toxins (scalded skin syndrome).

MRSA: Methicillin-resistant S. aureus carries the mecA gene encoding PBP2a (altered penicillin-binding protein), conferring resistance to all beta-lactams.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Catalase-positive (vs Streptococci), coagulase test, protein A function, TSST-1, PVL association with necrotizing pneumonia, MRSA mechanism (mecA/PBP2a), mannitol salt agar, and novobiocin resistance for S. saprophyticus.

Buzzwords: "Grape-like clusters," "catalase-positive cocci," "coagulase-positive," "golden-yellow colonies," "protein A."

Differential: Streptococci (catalase-negative, chains), Enterococci (catalase-negative, grow in bile/6.5% NaCl).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Gram-positive cocci in clusters, catalase-positive
  • Coagulase test: S. aureus = positive; S. epidermidis and S. saprophyticus = negative
  • S. aureus grows on mannitol salt agar as yellow (mannitol-fermenting) colonies
  • Protein A binds IgG Fc → inhibits opsonization and phagocytosis
  • Key toxins: TSST-1, enterotoxins (preformed → food poisoning), exfoliative toxin, PVL
  • MRSA: mecA gene → PBP2a → resistant to all beta-lactams; treat with vancomycin
  • S. epidermidis = biofilm producer, prosthetic device infections
  • S. saprophyticus = UTI in young sexually active women, novobiocin-resistant

  • What selective/differential medium is used for Staphylococci?
  • What gene confers methicillin resistance in MRSA and what protein does it encode?
  • Which coagulase-negative Staph species is novobiocin-resistant?
  • What toxin causes staphylococcal toxic shock syndrome?
  • Why is S. epidermidis a major concern in hospital settings?
6 · Additional Insights / Tips

The catalase test is a great first fork in the road: bubbles (catalase-positive) = Staph, no bubbles = Strep. Then coagulase splits Staph aureus from the rest. For the coagulase-negative species, remember: Saprophyticus = Sexually active women + UTIs, Novobiocin resistant = No. S. epidermidis sticks to epidermis and plastic (prosthetics, catheters) via biofilm.

S. aureus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Staphylococcus aureus is a Gram-positive, coagulase-positive coccus that grows in grape-like clusters. It is the most virulent Staphylococcus species and one of the most clinically significant bacteria in medicine. It colonizes the anterior nares and skin of approximately 30% of the population.

Why it matters: S. aureus causes a vast range of diseases from superficial skin infections (impetigo, folliculitis, abscesses) to life-threatening invasive infections (bacteremia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, pneumonia). It also produces toxin-mediated diseases: food poisoning, toxic shock syndrome, and scalded skin syndrome.

Key characteristics: Catalase-positive, coagulase-positive, beta-hemolytic, ferments mannitol (yellow on MSA), produces protein A, golden-yellow pigmented colonies. Facultative anaerobe.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Virulence factors: Protein A (binds IgG Fc, prevents opsonization), coagulase (fibrin clot formation around bacteria for protection), catalase (neutralizes H₂O₂ from neutrophils), alpha-toxin (pore-forming, lyses cells), PVL (destroys neutrophils, associated with CA-MRSA and necrotizing pneumonia), enterotoxins A–E (superantigens, preformed in food → emesis), TSST-1 (superantigen → toxic shock), exfoliative toxins A & B (cleave desmoglein-1 in epidermis → scalded skin syndrome).

Superantigens: TSST-1 and enterotoxins cross-link MHC II on APCs with TCR on T cells non-specifically, causing massive T-cell activation, cytokine storm, shock, and multiorgan failure.

Resistance: Beta-lactamase (penicillinase) → resistant to penicillin. mecA gene (PBP2a) → MRSA, resistant to all beta-lactams. VRSA is rare but emerging.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Toxin-mediated diseases (which toxin causes which syndrome), protein A mechanism, MRSA mechanism, superantigen concept, food poisoning (preformed enterotoxin — short incubation 1–6 hrs, no fever), acute endocarditis on normal valves.

Buzzwords: "Golden-yellow colonies," "acute endocarditis," "superantigen," "preformed toxin," "necrotizing pneumonia post-influenza."

Differential: S. epidermidis (subacute endocarditis, prosthetics), S. lugdunensis (aggressive CoNS), Strep pyogenes (also causes skin infections but is catalase-negative).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Gram-positive cocci in clusters, coagulase-positive, catalase-positive
  • Protein A binds Fc region of IgG → prevents opsonization
  • Superantigens (TSST-1, enterotoxins): cross-link MHC II + TCR → massive cytokine release
  • Food poisoning: preformed enterotoxin, rapid onset (1–6 hrs), vomiting predominant, no fever
  • Scalded skin syndrome: exfoliative toxin cleaves desmoglein-1
  • Acute bacterial endocarditis: S. aureus on previously NORMAL valves
  • MRSA: mecA gene → altered PBP2a → beta-lactam resistance
  • Post-influenza pneumonia: think S. aureus (necrotizing, PVL-positive strains)

  • How do superantigens like TSST-1 activate the immune system differently from normal antigens?
  • Why is S. aureus food poisoning characterized by rapid onset and vomiting?
  • What epidermal protein is cleaved by exfoliative toxin?
  • What structural change does PBP2a confer that makes MRSA resistant to beta-lactams?
  • What type of endocarditis does S. aureus typically cause — acute or subacute?
  • What is the role of Panton-Valentine leukocidin?
  • How does protein A help S. aureus evade the immune system?
6 · Additional Insights / Tips

S. aureus is the "golden" Staph — golden colonies, golden-yellow pigment. It's essentially the most aggressive Staph, and any question with "acute endocarditis on a normal valve" or "food poisoning with vomiting in 2 hours" is screaming S. aureus. For toxins, think: TSST-1 = Toxic Shock = Superantigen = Tampons. Exfoliative = "ex-foliates" the skin. Enterotoxin = enters fast, leaves fast (preformed).

Streptococci
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Streptococci are Gram-positive cocci that arrange in chains or pairs. They are catalase-negative, which distinguishes them from Staphylococci. Classified by hemolysis pattern on blood agar (alpha, beta, gamma) and by Lancefield grouping (carbohydrate antigens: A, B, D, etc.).

Why it matters: Streptococci cause pharyngitis, skin infections, pneumonia, meningitis, neonatal sepsis, urinary infections, and post-infectious sequelae like rheumatic fever and glomerulonephritis. Group A Strep (S. pyogenes) and Group B Strep (S. agalactiae) are especially high-yield.

Key characteristics: Catalase-negative, classified by hemolysis: alpha-hemolytic (partial, green zone — S. pneumoniae, viridans group), beta-hemolytic (complete, clear zone — Group A and B), gamma (no hemolysis — Enterococci, Group D).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Group A Strep (S. pyogenes): Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-sensitive, PYR-positive. Causes pharyngitis, impetigo, cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis, scarlet fever. Post-infectious: rheumatic fever (Jones criteria), post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis. Virulence: M protein (anti-phagocytic, basis for molecular mimicry with cardiac tissue), streptolysin O (ASO titer), hyaluronidase, streptokinase.

Group B Strep (S. agalactiae): Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-resistant, hippurate hydrolysis positive, CAMP test positive. Major cause of neonatal meningitis and sepsis. Screen pregnant women at 35–37 weeks.

S. pneumoniae: Alpha-hemolytic, lancet-shaped diplococci, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble, polysaccharide capsule (major virulence factor). Causes pneumonia (rust-colored sputum), meningitis, otitis media, sinusitis.

Viridans group: Alpha-hemolytic, optochin-resistant, bile-insoluble. Normal oral flora. Cause subacute bacterial endocarditis on damaged valves (S. mutans = dental caries).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Hemolysis patterns, bacitracin vs optochin sensitivity, M protein and rheumatic fever, Jones criteria, ASO titers, GBS screening in pregnancy, S. pneumoniae capsule, quellung reaction.

Buzzwords: "Chains," "catalase-negative," "bacitracin-sensitive" (GAS), "optochin-sensitive" (pneumo), "lancet-shaped diplococci," "rust-colored sputum."

Differential: Staphylococci (catalase-positive, clusters), Enterococci (Group D, grow in bile/6.5% NaCl), Viridans vs Pneumococcus (optochin, bile solubility).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Gram-positive cocci in chains, catalase-negative
  • Classified by hemolysis (alpha/beta/gamma) and Lancefield group (A, B, D…)
  • GAS (S. pyogenes): beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-sensitive, PYR+, M protein
  • M protein mimics cardiac myosin → rheumatic fever (molecular mimicry)
  • Rheumatic fever follows pharyngitis (NOT skin infection); PSGN can follow both
  • GBS (S. agalactiae): beta-hemolytic, CAMP+, neonatal meningitis/sepsis, screen at 35–37 wks
  • S. pneumoniae: alpha-hemolytic, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble, capsule = main virulence factor
  • Viridans strep: alpha-hemolytic, optochin-resistant, subacute endocarditis on DAMAGED valves

  • What hemolysis pattern does S. pyogenes show on blood agar?
  • What is M protein and what post-infectious complication does it contribute to?
  • Which Lancefield group is S. agalactiae and what neonatal infections does it cause?
  • How do you distinguish S. pneumoniae from viridans streptococci in the lab?

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

The classification tree is everything here: Catalase splits Staph from Strep. Hemolysis splits alpha from beta. Then specific tests split within: bacitracin (A vs B in beta), optochin (pneumo vs viridans in alpha). Mnemonic: B-BRAS — Bacitracin-sensitive = group B? No! Bacitracin-sensitive = group A (S. pyogenes). Think "A students are sensitive." Optochin = "O-P-tochin" = "O" and "P" go together = S. Pneumoniae is Optochin-sensitive.

Enterobacteria
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Enterobacteriaceae is a large family of Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic bacilli (rods) that inhabit the gastrointestinal tract. Major genera include Escherichia, Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterobacter, Serratia, Salmonella, Shigella, and Yersinia. They are oxidase-negative (key distinguishing feature from Pseudomonas) and ferment glucose.

Why it matters: They are the most common cause of urinary tract infections, many nosocomial infections, Gram-negative bacteremia and sepsis, and enteric infections (diarrhea, dysentery, typhoid fever). They are also a major reservoir for antibiotic resistance genes.

Key characteristics: Gram-negative rods, oxidase-negative, catalase-positive, grow on MacConkey agar. Classified as lactose fermenters (pink colonies on MacConkey: E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter) or non-lactose fermenters (colorless colonies: Salmonella, Shigella, Proteus).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Lactose fermenters vs non-fermenters: Lactose fermenters produce acid → pink/red colonies on MacConkey agar. Mnemonic for fast lactose fermenters: Klebsiella, E. coli, Enterobacter — "KEE on lac." Non-lactose fermenters: Salmonella, Shigella, Proteus.

Key virulence factors: Endotoxin (LPS/lipid A — present in ALL Gram-negatives), capsule (Klebsiella), flagella (motility — Salmonella, E. coli, Proteus), pili/fimbriae (adhesion), type III secretion systems (inject toxins into host cells).

Antibiotic resistance: ESBLs (extended-spectrum beta-lactamases) common in Klebsiella and E. coli. Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae (CRE) are an urgent public health threat.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Lactose fermentation on MacConkey agar, oxidase-negative status, LPS/endotoxin, specific pathogens and their disease associations, ESBLs and CRE.

Buzzwords: "Gram-negative rods," "oxidase-negative," "MacConkey agar," "lactose fermenter," "pink colonies."

Differential: Pseudomonas (oxidase-POSITIVE, non-fermenter), Vibrio (oxidase-positive, comma-shaped).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Gram-negative rods, facultative anaerobes, oxidase-negative
  • All have LPS (endotoxin) in outer membrane — activates TLR4, causes sepsis/septic shock
  • MacConkey agar: lactose fermenters = pink; non-lactose fermenters = colorless
  • Fast lactose fermenters: E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter
  • Non-lactose fermenters: Salmonella, Shigella, Proteus
  • Oxidase-negative distinguishes Enterobacteriaceae from Pseudomonas (oxidase+)
  • ESBLs and CRE are major resistance concerns
  • Most common cause of UTIs (E. coli), nosocomial infections, Gram-negative sepsis
5 · Active Recall Prompts
  • What key biochemical test distinguishes Enterobacteriaceae from Pseudomonas?
  • What does a pink colony on MacConkey agar indicate?
  • Name three lactose-fermenting Enterobacteriaceae.
  • Name three non-lactose-fermenting Enterobacteriaceae.
  • What is the endotoxin component of the Gram-negative outer membrane?
  • What is an ESBL and which organisms commonly carry them?
  • What immune receptor recognizes LPS?
  • Why do all Enterobacteriaceae have clinical significance in terms of sepsis?
6 · Additional Insights / Tips

MacConkey agar is your best friend for Enterobacteriaceae. It's selective (bile salts and crystal violet inhibit Gram-positives) and differential (lactose fermentation). Remember: "lactose = pink" because fermenting lactose produces acid → pH indicator turns pink. Non-lactose fermenters stay pale/colorless. The oxidase test is also critical: almost every Enterobacteriaceae is oxidase-negative. If it's oxidase-positive and a Gram-negative rod, think Pseudomonas instead.

E. coli
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Escherichia coli is a Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic rod and the most common Gram-negative bacterium in the normal intestinal flora. While most strains are harmless commensals, several pathogenic strains (pathotypes) cause significant disease. It is also a lactose fermenter — produces pink colonies on MacConkey agar and shows a green metallic sheen on EMB (eosin-methylene blue) agar.

Why it matters: E. coli is the #1 cause of urinary tract infections (UTIs), a leading cause of neonatal meningitis (K1 capsule), the most common cause of Gram-negative sepsis, a common cause of traveler's diarrhea (ETEC), and a cause of hemolytic uremic syndrome (EHEC O157:H7).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Pathotypes of diarrheagenic E. coli:

  • ETEC (Enterotoxigenic): produces heat-labile (LT) and heat-stable (ST) toxins. LT activates adenylate cyclase (↑cAMP) — similar to cholera toxin. ST activates guanylate cyclase (↑cGMP). Causes watery (traveler's) diarrhea.
  • EHEC (Enterohemorrhagic): O157:H7 serotype. Produces Shiga-like toxin (Stx). Causes bloody diarrhea, can progress to HUS (hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, renal failure). Do NOT treat with antibiotics (increases toxin release).
  • EIEC (Enteroinvasive): invades colonic mucosa, causes dysentery (like Shigella).
  • EPEC (Enteropathogenic): attaching and effacing lesions on intestinal epithelium. Diarrhea in infants.
  • EAEC (Enteroaggregative): "stacked-brick" adherence pattern, persistent diarrhea.

UTI strains: Uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) use P-fimbriae (pyelonephritis-associated pili) to adhere to uroepithelial cells.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: E. coli pathotypes and their toxin mechanisms, EHEC/HUS association, LT vs ST toxin mechanisms, K1 capsule and neonatal meningitis, green metallic sheen on EMB agar, #1 cause of UTIs.

Buzzwords: "Green metallic sheen on EMB," "traveler's diarrhea," "bloody diarrhea → HUS," "heat-labile / heat-stable toxin," "K1 capsule."

Differential: Shigella (also causes dysentery, Shiga toxin — but non-motile, non-lactose-fermenting), Salmonella (non-lactose fermenter), Klebsiella (lactose fermenter but heavily encapsulated).

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Gram-negative rod, lactose fermenter, green metallic sheen on EMB agar
  • #1 cause of UTIs (UPEC with P-fimbriae), Gram-negative sepsis, neonatal meningitis (K1 capsule)
  • ETEC: LT (↑cAMP like cholera) + ST (↑cGMP) → watery traveler's diarrhea
  • EHEC O157:H7: Shiga-like toxin → bloody diarrhea → HUS; do NOT treat with antibiotics
  • EIEC: invades mucosa → dysentery (Shigella-like)
  • EPEC: attaching & effacing → infant diarrhea
  • HUS triad: microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute renal failure
  • LT toxin = Labile to heat = activates adenyLate cyclase; ST toxin = Stable to heat = activates guanylate cyclase

  • What is the triad of hemolytic uremic syndrome?

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

The toxin mnemonics are gold: Labile = Like cholera = adenyLate cyclase = ↑cAMP. Stable = guanylate cyclase = ↑cGMP. For EHEC, remember: antibiotics lyse bacteria → release MORE Shiga-like toxin → WORSE outcome (HUS). The pathotypes spell out nicely: ETEC = Traveler's, EHEC = Hemorrhagic, EIEC = Invasive, EPEC = Pediatric.

Salmonella spp
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Salmonella is a genus of Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic rods belonging to Enterobacteriaceae. Two species are clinically relevant: S. enterica (with many serovars including Typhi, Paratyphi, Typhimurium, Enteritidis) and S. bongori. Neisseria gonorrhoeae

What it is: Neisseria gonorrhoeae (gonococcus) is a Gram-negative, kidney bean-shaped diplococcus. It is an obligate human pathogen — no animal reservoir. Listeria monocytogenes
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Listeria monocytogenes is a Gram-positive, facultatively anaerobic rod (bacillus) that is motile at room temperature via tumbling motility (actin rockets at 37°C inside cells). It is beta-hemolytic, catalase-positive, and a facultative intracellular organism — it can survive and replicate inside macrophages and other host cells.

Why it matters: Causes listeriosis — meningitis and septicemia in neonates, the elderly, immunocompromised, and pregnant women. It can cross the placenta (transplacental transmission) causing spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, or neonatal infection. It is notable for being able to grow at refrigerator temperatures (4°C — cold enrichment).

Key characteristics: Transmitted via contaminated deli meats, unpasteurized dairy (soft cheeses), hot dogs. One of the few Gram-positive organisms with LPS-like activity. Tumbling motility at 25°C.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Intracellular lifecycle: Listeria enters cells via internalin receptors → escapes the phagosome using listeriolysin O (LLO, a pore-forming hemolysin) → replicates freely in the cytoplasm → uses ActA to polymerize host actin ("actin rockets") → propels itself into adjacent cells, spreading cell-to-cell without exposure to the extracellular immune system.

Immunity: Because it's intracellular, cell-mediated immunity (T cells) is essential for clearance. This explains why it targets immunocompromised patients (especially those with impaired T-cell function) and why antibodies alone are insufficient.

Lab: Gram-positive rod, beta-hemolytic, catalase-positive, tumbling motility at 25°C, grows at 4°C.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Intracellular lifecycle (actin rockets, listeriolysin O), growth at 4°C, transplacental transmission, meningitis in neonates/elderly/immunocompromised/pregnant, contaminated deli meats and unpasteurized dairy.

Vibrio cholerae
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Vibrio cholerae is a Gram-negative, comma-shaped (curved) rod that is oxidase-positive and motile with a single polar flagellum. It causes cholera, an acute diarrheal disease that can lead to massive fluid loss and death within hours if untreated. The clinically important serogroups are O1 and O139.

Why it matters: Cholera remains a major global health threat, especially in areas with poor sanitation and after natural disasters. The disease causes profuse "rice-water" watery diarrhea (up to 20 liters/day) that can rapidly lead to hypovolemic shock and death.

Key characteristics: Transmitted via fecally contaminated water. Grows on TCBS (thiosulfate citrate bile salts sucrose) agar as yellow colonies. Produces cholera toxin (CT), an AB toxin with ADP-ribosyltransferase activity.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Cholera toxin mechanism: The B subunit binds to GM1 ganglioside on intestinal epithelial cells. NOTE: the toxin does NOT damage the mucosa — no blood, no inflammation, no WBCs in stool.

Clinical: Severe watery "rice-water" diarrhea (flecks of mucus), vomiting, rapid dehydration. No fever, no blood in stool. Death from dehydration/electrolyte imbalance if not treated with aggressive fluid resuscitation (ORS is life-saving).

Lab: Oxidase-positive comma-shaped rod, TCBS agar (yellow colonies), darting motility ("shooting star") in hanging drop prep.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Cholera toxin mechanism (ADP-ribosylation of Gsα → ↑cAMP), rice-water stool, GM1 ganglioside binding, comparison with E. coli LT toxin (same mechanism), TCBS agar.

Differential: ETEC (similar cAMP mechanism but less severe), other Vibrio species (V. parahaemolyticus — seafood, V. vulnificus — wound infections in liver disease).

Why it matters: C. difficile infection (CDI) is a major hospital-acquired infection with high recurrence rates. C. perfringens gas gangrene is a surgical emergency. Both are high-yield for understanding anaerobic infections, toxin-mediated disease, and spore biology.

Bacteria — Virulence / Pathogenicity / Biofilms
Pili
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Pili (also called fimbriae) are thin, hair-like protein appendages on the surface of bacteria, primarily Gram-negative organisms. They are composed of pilin protein subunits. Their primary function is adhesion to host cells and surfaces, which is essential for colonization and the initiation of infection.

Why it matters: Without pili, many bacteria cannot adhere to mucosal surfaces and would be cleared by mucosal flow (urine, secretions). Pili are therefore critical virulence factors. Special types of pili (sex pili / F pili) also mediate conjugation — the horizontal transfer of genetic material between bacteria, including antibiotic resistance genes.

Key characteristics: Shorter and thinner than flagella. Classified into common pili (adhesion) and sex pili (conjugation). Many pili show antigenic variation, helping bacteria evade the immune system.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Types: (1) Common (Type I) pili — mediate adhesion. E. coli P-fimbriae bind to uroepithelial cells (important in UTIs and pyelonephritis). N. gonorrhoeae type IV pili mediate attachment and undergo antigenic variation. (2) Sex pili (F pili) — encoded by F (fertility) plasmid, form a conjugation bridge between bacteria for DNA transfer.

Antigenic variation: N. gonorrhoeae and N. meningitidis can alter the pilin gene through recombination, changing the pili's antigenic structure. This prevents the host from mounting effective antibody responses and is a key reason no vaccine exists for gonorrhea.

Clinical relevance: Pili are essential for uropathogenic E. coli, N. gonorrhoeae, V. cholerae (toxin-coregulated pili), Bordetella pertussis, and many other pathogens.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Function of pili (adhesion vs conjugation), P-fimbriae and UTIs, type IV pili and antigenic variation in Neisseria, sex pili and conjugation, pili vs flagella.

Buzzwords: "Fimbriae," "pilin subunits," "adhesion to host cells," "conjugation bridge," "antigenic variation."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Pili = thin protein appendages, primarily for adhesion to host cells/surfaces
  • Made of pilin protein subunits
  • Common pili = adhesion; sex pili (F pili) = conjugation (DNA transfer)
  • P-fimbriae of E. coli → adherence to urinary tract → UTIs/pyelonephritis
  • Type IV pili of N. gonorrhoeae → attachment + antigenic variation → immune evasion
  • Sex pili are encoded by F plasmid → conjugation bridge for horizontal gene transfer
  • Pili ≠ flagella: pili = attachment/DNA transfer; flagella = motility
  • Antigenic variation of pili = reason N. gonorrhoeae has no vaccine

Biofilm
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A biofilm is a structured community of bacteria embedded in a self-produced extracellular polymeric substance (EPS) matrix made of polysaccharides, proteins, and extracellular DNA. Biofilm bacteria attach to surfaces (biological or inert) and behave fundamentally differently from free-floating (planktonic) bacteria.

Why it matters: Biofilms are extremely resistant to antibiotics (up to 1000x more resistant than planktonic bacteria) and host immune defenses. They are responsible for chronic/recurrent infections, especially those involving prosthetic devices (catheters, prosthetic joints, heart valves, endotracheal tubes).

Key characteristics: Formed in stages: (1) initial attachment via pili/adhesins, (2) microcolony formation, (3) EPS matrix production, (4) maturation into 3D architecture with water channels, (5) dispersal of planktonic cells to colonize new sites.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Why biofilms resist antibiotics: EPS matrix acts as a physical barrier, preventing antibiotic penetration. Bacteria in biofilms have reduced metabolic activity (many antibiotics target actively dividing cells). Biofilms contain persister cells — dormant variants tolerant to antibiotics. The biofilm also creates microenvironments (pH gradients, oxygen gradients) that may inactivate some drugs.

Clinically important biofilm producers: S. epidermidis (prosthetic devices, catheters), Pseudomonas aeruginosa (cystic fibrosis lungs, ventilators), S. aureus, E. coli (urinary catheters), Streptococcus mutans (dental plaque).

Quorum sensing: Bacteria within biofilms communicate via chemical signaling molecules (autoinducers). When a threshold concentration is reached, gene expression changes collectively — coordinating biofilm formation, virulence factor production, and dispersal.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Why biofilms are resistant to antibiotics, association with prosthetic device infections (S. epidermidis is classic), quorum sensing concept, stages of biofilm formation.

Buzzwords: "Extracellular polymeric substance," "prosthetic device infection," "quorum sensing," "persister cells."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Biofilm = community of bacteria in self-produced EPS matrix on surfaces
  • Stages: attachment → microcolony → EPS production → maturation → dispersal
  • Up to 1000x more antibiotic-resistant than planktonic (free-floating) bacteria
  • Resistance mechanisms: physical barrier (EPS), reduced metabolism, persister cells
  • Key biofilm producers: S. epidermidis, Pseudomonas, S. aureus, E. coli, S. mutans
  • S. epidermidis = classic prosthetic device/catheter biofilm infection
  • Quorum sensing = bacterial cell-to-cell communication via autoinducers
  • Often requires device removal because antibiotics alone cannot clear biofilm

Plasmid
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A plasmid is a small, circular, double-stranded, extrachromosomal DNA molecule that replicates independently of the bacterial chromosome. Plasmids carry non-essential but advantageous genes — most importantly, antibiotic resistance genes, virulence factors, and genes for metabolic functions.

Why it matters: Plasmids are the primary vehicles for horizontal gene transfer of antibiotic resistance between bacteria. The rapid spread of multidrug-resistant organisms (MRSA, ESBL-producing E. coli, CRE) is largely driven by plasmid-mediated resistance gene transfer via conjugation.

Key characteristics: Self-replicating (has an origin of replication), transferable between bacteria (via conjugation if the plasmid carries tra genes), can carry multiple resistance genes simultaneously (R plasmids). Some plasmids integrate into the chromosome (episomes).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Types of plasmids: (1) F plasmid (fertility) — encodes sex pilus, mediates conjugation. (2) R plasmid (resistance) — carries antibiotic resistance genes (e.g., beta-lactamase, aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes). (3) Virulence plasmids — encode toxins or other virulence factors (e.g., heat-labile/heat-stable toxins of ETEC). (4) Col plasmids — produce bacteriocins. (5) Metabolic plasmids — encode enzymes for degrading unusual substances.

Clinical significance: A single R plasmid can carry genes for resistance to multiple antibiotic classes simultaneously, creating multidrug-resistant organisms in one transfer event. Plasmids spread between species and even genera, making containment of resistance extremely difficult.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Plasmids as extrachromosomal DNA, R plasmids and resistance transfer, F plasmid and conjugation, difference between plasmids and chromosomal DNA, horizontal gene transfer.

Buzzwords: "Extrachromosomal DNA," "self-replicating," "R factor," "conjugation," "horizontal gene transfer."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Plasmid = small, circular, extrachromosomal dsDNA, self-replicating
  • Carry non-essential but advantageous genes: resistance, virulence, metabolism
  • R plasmids carry antibiotic resistance genes — major driver of MDR spread
  • F plasmid encodes sex pilus → conjugation (direct cell-to-cell DNA transfer)
  • Plasmids can carry multiple resistance genes simultaneously
  • Transferred horizontally via conjugation, also can transfer by transformation/transduction
  • Episomes = plasmids that can integrate into the chromosome
  • Not essential for bacterial survival but provide selective advantage under pressure (e.g., antibiotics)

Transformation / Transduction
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Transformation and transduction are two of the three main mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in bacteria (the third being conjugation). Transformation is the uptake of free (naked) DNA from the environment by a competent bacterium. Transduction is the transfer of bacterial DNA from one bacterium to another via a bacteriophage (bacterial virus).

Why it matters: HGT allows bacteria to acquire new genes — including antibiotic resistance, virulence factors, and metabolic capabilities — without vertical inheritance. This drives bacterial evolution and the rapid emergence of resistant and virulent strains.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Transformation: Bacteria must be "competent" (able to take up DNA) — some are naturally competent (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae), others require artificial induction (CaCl₂ treatment, electroporation). Griffith's experiment (1928) demonstrated transformation using S. pneumoniae (rough ↔ smooth). Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty later proved that DNA was the transforming principle.

Transduction — two types: (1) Generalized transduction: any bacterial DNA can be accidentally packaged into a phage head during lytic cycle → transferred to a new host. (2) Specialized transduction: only genes adjacent to the prophage insertion site are transferred when a lysogenic phage excises imprecisely. Classic example: the gene for diphtheria toxin is carried by a lysogenic phage (corynephage β) → only lysogenized C. diphtheriae strains produce the toxin.

Conjugation (for comparison): Requires direct cell-to-cell contact via sex pilus (F pilus), transfers plasmid or chromosomal DNA. Most efficient method of HGT.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Griffith's experiment, types of transduction, lysogenic conversion (toxin genes carried by phages — diphtheria, cholera, botulinum, Shiga toxin, erythrogenic toxin of Strep pyogenes), differences between transformation/transduction/conjugation.

  • Griffith's experiment: heat-killed smooth + live rough S. pneumoniae → smooth (transformation)

  • Lysogenic conversion: phage genes give bacteria new virulence (diphtheria toxin, cholera toxin, botulinum toxin, Shiga toxin, erythrogenic toxin)

Bacteria — Cultures / Lab Identification
Urine culture
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A urine culture is the gold standard microbiological test for diagnosing urinary tract infections. It involves collecting a urine specimen (ideally a clean-catch midstream sample or catheterized specimen), inoculating it onto culture media (blood agar, MacConkey agar, CLED agar), and incubating to identify the causative organism and quantify colony counts.

Why it matters: UTIs are among the most common bacterial infections. Urine culture confirms the diagnosis, identifies the pathogen, provides antibiotic sensitivities (AST), and helps distinguish true infection from contamination.

Key characteristics: Significant bacteriuria is generally defined as ≥10⁵ CFU/mL (100,000 colonies/mL) for a clean-catch midstream specimen. Lower counts (≥10² CFU/mL) may be significant in catheterized specimens or symptomatic patients.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Common UTI pathogens: E. coli (#1 cause, ~80% of uncomplicated UTIs), Staphylococcus saprophyticus (young sexually active women, novobiocin-resistant), Klebsiella pneumoniae, Proteus mirabilis (alkaline urine, struvite stones, "swarming" motility), Enterococcus faecalis, Pseudomonas aeruginosa (catheter-associated).

Collection methods: Clean-catch midstream (most common, reduces contamination), catheterized specimen, suprapubic aspiration (gold standard, any growth is significant). Contamination clues: multiple organisms, squamous epithelial cells present, low colony counts.

Media: CLED (cystine lactose electrolyte deficient) agar is commonly used — prevents swarming of Proteus while allowing lactose differentiation.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Significant bacteriuria threshold (≥10⁵ CFU/mL), most common UTI organisms, collection methods and contamination, Proteus and struvite stones.

Buzzwords: "≥10⁵ CFU/mL," "clean-catch midstream," "swarming motility," "struvite stones."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)

  • Significant bacteriuria: ≥10⁵ CFU/mL (clean-catch midstream)
  • E. coli = #1 UTI pathogen (~80% uncomplicated)

Blood culture
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A blood culture is the collection and incubation of blood in specialized culture bottles to detect bacteremia (bacteria in blood) or fungemia. Typically, two sets are drawn from separate venipuncture sites, each set consisting of an aerobic and an anaerobic bottle. Modern labs use automated continuous-monitoring systems (e.g., BACTEC, BacT/ALERT) that detect microbial growth by sensing CO₂ production.

Why it matters: Bloodstream infections (sepsis/bacteremia) carry high mortality. Blood cultures are essential for identifying the causative organism, determining antibiotic susceptibility, and guiding targeted therapy. They are also critical for diagnosing infective endocarditis (Duke criteria require positive blood cultures).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Procedure: Strict aseptic technique to minimize contamination (skin antisepsis with chlorhexidine). Draw before antibiotics whenever possible. Two sets from two sites to help distinguish true bacteremia (same organism in both) from contamination (only one positive, common skin flora).

Common true pathogens: S. aureus (almost always significant), E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, S. pneumoniae, Enterococcus, Candida. Common contaminants: coagulase-negative Staphylococci (S. epidermidis), Corynebacterium, Cutibacterium acnes — though may be true pathogens in prosthetic device infections.

Time to positivity: Most true pathogens grow within 24–48 hours. Fastidious organisms (HACEK group, Brucella) may take longer (5–7+ days).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: When to draw blood cultures (before antibiotics), interpreting true positive vs contamination, two sets from two sites, S. aureus bacteremia always significant.

Buzzwords: "Two sets, two sites," "before antibiotics," "continuous-monitoring system," "aerobic and anaerobic bottles."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)

  • Automated systems (BACTEC, BacT/ALERT) detect CO₂ from microbial metabolism
  • Most pathogens detected in 24–48 hours; fastidious organisms take longer
  • Essential for diagnosing endocarditis (Duke criteria)

CSF culture and analysis
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) culture and analysis involves performing a lumbar puncture (LP) to collect CSF, then analyzing it for cells, chemistry (glucose, protein), Gram stain, culture, and sometimes special tests (PCR, latex agglutination, India ink). It is the definitive diagnostic test for meningitis and encephalitis.

Why it matters: Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency with high mortality if untreated. CSF analysis allows rapid differentiation between bacterial, viral, fungal, and TB meningitis, guiding immediate therapy.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

CSF patterns:

  • Bacterial meningitis: ↑↑ WBCs (neutrophil predominance), ↑↑ protein, ↓↓ glucose (<40 mg/dL or <2/3 of serum glucose), positive Gram stain/culture.
  • Viral meningitis: ↑ WBCs (lymphocyte predominance), normal-to-mildly ↑ protein, normal glucose, negative Gram stain/culture.
  • TB/Fungal meningitis: ↑ WBCs (lymphocyte predominance), ↑↑ protein, ↓ glucose, negative routine culture (need AFB/fungal culture).

Common organisms by age: Neonates: GBS, E. coli, Listeria. Children (3 mo–18 yr): N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae. Adults: S. pneumoniae (#1), N. meningitidis. Elderly/immunocompromised: S. pneumoniae, Listeria, Gram-negative rods.

Special tests: India ink for Cryptococcus neoformans. Latex agglutination for capsular antigens. PCR (especially for HSV encephalitis, N. meningitidis).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: CSF patterns (bacterial vs viral vs TB/fungal), organisms by age group, India ink, significance of low glucose in CSF.

Buzzwords: "Neutrophilic pleocytosis," "low glucose," "lymphocytic pleocytosis," "India ink positive."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Lumbar puncture with CSF analysis = gold standard for meningitis diagnosis
  • Bacterial: ↑neutrophils, ↑protein, ↓glucose, positive Gram stain/culture
  • Viral: ↑lymphocytes, normal/↑protein, normal glucose, negative culture
  • TB/Fungal: ↑lymphocytes, ↑protein, ↓glucose
  • Neonates: GBS, E. coli K1, Listeria
  • Adults: S. pneumoniae (#1), N. meningitidis
  • India ink = Cryptococcus; latex agglutination detects capsular antigens
  • CSF glucose <40 mg/dL or <2/3 serum glucose = suggestive of bacterial/TB/fungal

Stool culture
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A stool culture involves collecting a fecal sample and inoculating it onto selective and differential media to identify enteric pathogens. The goal is to isolate pathogenic bacteria from the vast normal flora of the GI tract.

Why it matters: Enteric infections cause significant morbidity and mortality worldwide. Stool culture identifies the causative organism, guides antibiotic therapy when needed, and is essential for public health surveillance of outbreaks.

Key characteristics: Uses selective media that suppress normal flora while allowing pathogens to grow. Key media include MacConkey agar, XLD (xylose lysine deoxycholate) agar, Hektoen enteric agar, SS (Salmonella-Shigella) agar, TCBS agar (for Vibrio), Campy-BAP (for Campylobacter at 42°C), and sorbitol-MacConkey (for EHEC O157:H7 — does not ferment sorbitol).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Major stool pathogens: Salmonella (non-lactose fermenter, H₂S+), Shigella (non-lactose fermenter, H₂S−, non-motile), Campylobacter (curved Gram-negative rod, grows at 42°C), E. coli O157:H7 (sorbitol-negative on SMAC), Vibrio (TCBS), C. difficile (anaerobic culture or toxin detection, not routine stool culture).

Lactose fermentation on MacConkey: Differentiates normal flora (E. coli = pink, lactose+) from pathogens (Salmonella, Shigella = colorless, lactose−).

Indicators of invasive/inflammatory diarrhea: Fecal WBCs (leukocytes), blood in stool, mucus — suggest Shigella, Salmonella, Campylobacter, EIEC, or C. difficile.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Selective media for specific organisms, lactose vs non-lactose fermenters, sorbitol-MacConkey for O157:H7, TCBS for Vibrio, Campy at 42°C.

Buzzwords: "Non-lactose fermenter," "colorless colonies," "H₂S production," "sorbitol-negative," "42°C incubation."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Selective media suppress normal flora to isolate enteric pathogens
  • MacConkey: lactose fermenters (pink) vs non-fermenters (colorless)
  • Salmonella: colorless on MacConkey, black (H₂S) on XLD/Hektoen
  • Shigella: colorless on MacConkey, NO H₂S, non-motile
  • EHEC O157:H7: does NOT ferment sorbitol → colorless on sorbitol-MacConkey
  • Vibrio: TCBS agar (yellow colonies)
  • Campylobacter: 42°C on Campy-BAP (microaerophilic)
  • C. difficile diagnosed by toxin assay/PCR, not routine stool culture

Diagnosis of typhoid fever
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Typhoid fever is a systemic infection caused by Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi. Diagnosis involves a combination of clinical features, blood culture (gold standard in the first week), stool culture (positive from week 3), bone marrow culture (most sensitive overall), and serological tests (Widal test).

Why it matters: Typhoid fever is endemic in many developing countries and can cause life-threatening complications (intestinal perforation, hemorrhage, encephalopathy). Accurate and timely diagnosis is critical for appropriate therapy and public health containment.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Diagnostic timeline: Week 1: blood culture most positive (bacteremia). Week 2: serology (Widal test) becomes positive. Week 3: stool culture becomes positive (organisms shed in feces). Bone marrow culture: most sensitive at any stage (~90% sensitivity even after antibiotics started).

Widal test: Detects antibodies against O (somatic) and H (flagellar) antigens of S. Typhi. A ≥4-fold rise in titer between acute and convalescent sera is significant. Limited by cross-reactivity, prior vaccination, and endemic area baseline titers.

Modern diagnostics: Blood culture remains gold standard. PCR and rapid antigen tests (Typhidot, Tubex) are increasingly used. Bone marrow culture is the most sensitive but invasive.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Blood culture in week 1, Widal test limitations, bone marrow culture sensitivity, stool culture timing.

Buzzwords: "Stepwise fever," "rose spots," "blood culture week 1," "Widal test," "bone marrow culture most sensitive."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Blood culture = gold standard, highest yield in week 1 (bacteremia phase)
  • Stool culture becomes positive from week 3 onward
  • Bone marrow culture = most sensitive test overall (~90%), positive even on antibiotics
  • Widal test: measures anti-O and anti-H antibodies; 4-fold rise is significant
  • Widal test limitations: cross-reactivity, prior vaccination, endemic baselines
  • Clinical clues: stepwise fever, relative bradycardia, rose spots, hepatosplenomegaly
  • Complications: intestinal perforation at Peyer's patches (terminal ileum), hemorrhage
  • Chronic carriers: gallbladder harbors S. Typhi

Chlamydia detection
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Chlamydia trachomatis is an obligate intracellular bacterium (requires host cells to replicate — cannot make its own ATP, the "energy parasite"). Detection methods include NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test — the gold standard), culture on McCoy cells, direct fluorescent antibody (DFA), and serology. Because Chlamydia cannot be grown on standard bacterial media, specialized detection methods are required.

Why it matters: Chlamydia trachomatis is the most common bacterial STI worldwide. Many infections are asymptomatic, leading to undetected transmission and complications: PID, ectopic pregnancy, infertility, reactive arthritis, and neonatal conjunctivitis/pneumonia. Screening and early detection are critical.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

NAAT: The current gold standard — highest sensitivity and specificity. Detects chlamydial DNA/RNA. Can be performed on urine, cervical/urethral swabs, or self-collected vaginal swabs. Does NOT require viable organisms.

Cell culture: McCoy cell culture was the historical gold standard but is now largely replaced by NAAT. The organism forms intracytoplasmic inclusions that can be visualized (contain reticulate bodies). Elementary bodies (EBs) are the infectious, extracellular form; reticulate bodies (RBs) are the intracellular, replicating form.

Lifecycle: EB (small, dense, infectious) attaches to host cell → endocytosis → EB converts to RB within inclusion body → RB replicates by binary fission → RBs convert back to EBs → cell lysis/exocytosis releases EBs to infect new cells.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Obligate intracellular organism (cannot make ATP), NAAT as gold standard, EB vs RB forms, cannot grow on standard media, intracytoplasmic inclusions, energy parasite.

Buzzwords: "Obligate intracellular," "elementary body," "reticulate body," "inclusion body," "energy parasite," "NAAT."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Chlamydia = obligate intracellular, cannot make own ATP ("energy parasite")
  • Cannot be cultured on standard bacterial media — requires cell culture (McCoy cells)
  • NAAT = current gold standard for detection (highest sensitivity/specificity)
  • Two forms: EB (elementary body = infectious, extracellular) and RB (reticulate body = replicating, intracellular)
  • Intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies visible on culture/staining
  • Most common bacterial STI worldwide — often asymptomatic
  • Serovars: A–C (trachoma/blindness), D–K (urogenital STI, neonatal), L1–L3 (lymphogranuloma venereum)

Viruses — DNA Viruses / Capsid
HPV
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Human papillomavirus (HPV) is a non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA virus with an icosahedral capsid. There are over 200 HPV types. It infects squamous epithelium (skin and mucous membranes). Low-risk types (6, 11) cause benign warts (condylomata acuminata); high-risk types (16, 18, 31, 33) cause cervical, oropharyngeal, anal, and other cancers.

Why it matters: HPV is the most common sexually transmitted infection. High-risk HPV causes virtually all cervical cancers, making screening (Pap smear, HPV DNA testing) and vaccination critical for prevention.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Oncogenic mechanism: High-risk HPV integrates into host DNA. E6 protein binds and degrades p53 (tumor suppressor) via ubiquitin-proteasome pathway. E7 protein binds and inactivates Rb (retinoblastoma protein), releasing E2F and driving uncontrolled cell proliferation. Together, E6 and E7 disable the two major tumor suppressors.

Key associations: Types 6, 11 = warts (condylomata), laryngeal papillomatosis. Types 16, 18 = ~70% of cervical cancers. Koilocytes (cells with perinuclear halo and nuclear irregularity) on Pap smear are pathognomonic for HPV infection.

Vaccine: 9-valent (Gardasil 9) covers types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52, 58.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: E6 degrades p53, E7 inactivates Rb, koilocytes, type 16/18 = cancer, type 6/11 = warts, non-enveloped dsDNA virus.

Buzzwords: "Koilocytes," "E6/E7 oncoproteins," "p53 degradation," "Rb inactivation," "condylomata acuminata."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Non-enveloped, dsDNA, icosahedral virus
  • Types 6, 11 = benign warts (condylomata acuminata, laryngeal papillomas)
  • Types 16, 18 = cervical cancer (and oropharyngeal, anal cancers)
  • E6 → degrades p53 (via ubiquitin ligase); E7 → inactivates Rb
  • Both tumor suppressors disabled → uncontrolled cell cycle → cancer
  • Koilocytes = hallmark cytological finding of HPV infection
  • Non-enveloped = resistant to drying, detergents, and alcohol
  • Gardasil 9 vaccine covers 9 types including high-risk and low-risk strains

Adenovirus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Adenovirus is a non-enveloped, double-stranded linear DNA virus with an icosahedral capsid. It has characteristic fiber proteins projecting from the vertices of the capsid (penton fibers). There are over 50 serotypes affecting humans.

Why it matters: Adenoviruses cause a wide range of infections: upper respiratory infections (common cold, pharyngitis), conjunctivitis ("pink eye," especially epidemic keratoconjunctivitis), gastroenteritis (serotypes 40, 41 in children), hemorrhagic cystitis, and pneumonia (especially in military recruits and immunocompromised).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Key features: Non-enveloped → survives on surfaces and in water (resistant to GI tract conditions — hence GI infections). Linear dsDNA. Febrile pharyngoconjunctival fever is a classic presentation (fever + pharyngitis + conjunctivitis). "Swimming pool conjunctivitis" in children. Pneumonia in military recruits (types 4, 7 — vaccine exists for military). Can cause hemorrhagic cystitis in immunocompromised (especially post-BMT).

Virology: Replicates in the nucleus. Can cause lytic infection or remain latent in lymphoid tissue (tonsils, adenoids — hence the name). Penton fibers mediate viral attachment to host cells.

Most commonly tested: Non-enveloped dsDNA, pharyngoconjunctival fever, swimming pool conjunctivitis, pneumonia in military recruits, gastroenteritis in children.

Buzzwords: "Non-enveloped DNA virus," "pharyngoconjunctival fever," "military recruits," "swimming pool conjunctivitis," "penton fibers."

Herpes virus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Herpesviridae is a family of large, enveloped, double-stranded linear DNA viruses. All herpesviruses establish lifelong latency after primary infection. The eight human herpesviruses are: HSV-1, HSV-2, VZV (HHV-3), EBV (HHV-4), CMV (HHV-5), HHV-6, HHV-7, HHV-8 (KSHV).

Why it matters: Herpesviruses cause a massive range of clinical disease — from cold sores to encephalitis to cancers. Their ability to establish latency and reactivate makes them clinically significant throughout a patient's lifetime, especially in immunocompromised individuals.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

HSV-1: Oral herpes, herpes labialis, temporal lobe encephalitis (most common cause of sporadic viral encephalitis). Latent in trigeminal ganglia.

HSV-2: Genital herpes, neonatal herpes. Latent in sacral ganglia.

VZV (HHV-3): Chickenpox (primary), shingles/zoster (reactivation in dermatomal distribution). Latent in dorsal root ganglia.

EBV (HHV-4): Infectious mononucleosis ("mono"), associated with Burkitt lymphoma, nasopharyngeal carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder. Infects B cells. Heterophile antibody positive (Monospot test).

CMV (HHV-5): Mono-like illness (heterophile-negative), congenital infection (TORCH), retinitis in AIDS. Owl-eye intranuclear inclusions.

HHV-6: Roseola infantum (exanthem subitum — high fever → rash when fever breaks).

HHV-8 (KSHV): Kaposi sarcoma (in AIDS patients).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Latency sites, HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe), EBV and associated cancers, CMV owl-eye inclusions, VZV dermatomal reactivation, Tzanck smear (multinucleated giant cells — HSV/VZV).

Buzzwords: "Latency," "temporal lobe encephalitis," "Monospot," "owl-eye inclusions," "dermatomal vesicles."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • All herpesviruses: enveloped, dsDNA, establish lifelong latency, reactivate
  • HSV-1: oral, encephalitis (temporal lobe), latent in trigeminal ganglia
  • HSV-2: genital, neonatal herpes, latent in sacral ganglia

HBV
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Hepatitis B virus (HBV) is a partially double-stranded (relaxed circular) DNA virus of the Hepadnaviridae family. It is enveloped and replicates via reverse transcriptase (despite being a DNA virus). It infects hepatocytes and can cause acute hepatitis, chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC).

Why it matters: HBV infects ~300 million people worldwide chronically. It is one of the most important causes of chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, and liver cancer. Vaccination is highly effective and is part of routine childhood immunization worldwide.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Serology (extremely high-yield):

  • HBsAg (surface antigen): first marker of infection; present = active infection (acute or chronic)
  • Anti-HBs (surface antibody): indicates immunity (from recovery or vaccination)
  • HBcAg (core antigen): not measurable in serum
  • Anti-HBc IgM: acute infection marker
  • Anti-HBc IgG: indicates prior exposure (present in recovered AND chronic)
  • HBeAg: marker of active viral replication and high infectivity
  • "Window period": HBsAg has cleared but anti-HBs hasn't appeared yet — only anti-HBc IgM is positive

Replication: Unique among DNA viruses — uses reverse transcriptase. This makes it susceptible to nucleoside/nucleotide analogs (tenofovir, entecavir).

Oncogenesis: HBV integration into host genome → genomic instability → HCC. Also indirect via chronic inflammation → cirrhosis → HCC.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Serological interpretation (the table of markers), window period, reverse transcriptase in a DNA virus, HCC association, vertical transmission.

Buzzwords: "Partially dsDNA," "reverse transcriptase," "Dane particle," "window period," "HBsAg."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Partially dsDNA, enveloped, Hepadnaviridae, uses reverse transcriptase
  • HBsAg = active infection marker; Anti-HBs = immunity
  • Anti-HBc IgM = acute infection; Anti-HBc IgG = prior exposure
  • HBeAg = high replication, high infectivity
  • Window period: HBsAg negative, Anti-HBs negative, Anti-HBc IgM positive
  • Vaccination produces Anti-HBs ONLY (no anti-HBc since no exposure to core)
  • Chronic HBV → cirrhosis → hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Transmitted by blood, sexual contact, vertical (mother-to-child)

Yellow fever virus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Yellow fever virus is an enveloped, single-stranded positive-sense RNA virus of the Flaviviridae family (genus Flavivirus). It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti mosquito (same vector as dengue, Zika, and chikungunya). Endemic in tropical Africa and South America.

Why it matters: Yellow fever causes a hemorrhagic fever with high case fatality rate (20–50% in severe cases). It targets the liver, causing jaundice (hence "yellow" fever), hepatic necrosis with Councilman bodies (eosinophilic apoptotic hepatocytes), and can progress to hemorrhagic manifestations, renal failure, and death.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Clinical phases: Infection → period of remission → "intoxication phase" (in severe cases): high fever, jaundice, hemorrhage (black vomit from GI bleeding), renal failure. Classic pathology: mid-zone hepatic necrosis (sparing of hepatocytes around central vein and portal tracts), Councilman bodies.

Vaccine: Live-attenuated 17D vaccine — highly effective, single dose gives long-term immunity. Required for travel to endemic areas. Contraindicated in immunocompromised, pregnant women, infants <6 months.

Vector: Aedes aegypti — urban cycle. Sylvatic (jungle) cycle involves non-human primates as reservoir.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Councilman bodies, mid-zone hepatic necrosis, Aedes aegypti vector, Flavivirus family, live-attenuated vaccine.

Buzzwords: "Councilman bodies," "mid-zone necrosis," "Aedes aegypti," "black vomit," "jaundice," "hemorrhagic fever."

  • Endemic in tropical Africa and South America (NOT Asia)

Pneumoviridae
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Pneumoviridae is a virus family (formerly classified within Paramyxoviridae) containing Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) and human metapneumovirus (hMPV). RSV is an enveloped, single-stranded negative-sense RNA virus. It has a helical nucleocapsid and is non-segmented.

Why it matters: RSV is the most common cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in infants and young children (<2 years). It is also a significant pathogen in the elderly and immunocompromised. RSV causes annual winter epidemics and is the #1 reason for infant hospitalization due to respiratory illness.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

RSV: F (fusion) protein is the key virulence factor — causes infected respiratory epithelial cells to fuse into multinucleated giant cells (syncytia, hence "syncytial" in the name). G protein mediates viral attachment. Transmitted by respiratory droplets and fomites.

Clinical: Infants present with bronchiolitis — wheezing, tachypnea, nasal flaring, retractions, hypoxia. Can progress to pneumonia. Re-infection throughout life (does not induce lasting immunity). In adults/elderly: URI or lower respiratory infection.

Diagnosis: Rapid antigen testing, PCR (gold standard), viral culture.

Prevention: Palivizumab (monoclonal antibody against F protein) for prophylaxis in high-risk infants (premature, cardiac/pulmonary disease). RSV vaccines now available for older adults and maternal vaccination.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: #1 cause of bronchiolitis in infants, F protein and syncytia formation, palivizumab prophylaxis, (−) ssRNA virus.

Buzzwords: "Syncytia," "bronchiolitis in infant," "F protein," "palivizumab," "winter epidemics."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)

  • Also significant in elderly and immunocompromised

Influenza virus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Influenza virus is an enveloped, single-stranded negative-sense RNA virus with a segmented genome (8 segments). It belongs to the Orthomyxoviridae family. Three types: A (most clinically significant, causes pandemics), B (causes epidemics, only infects humans), and C (mild URI, not clinically significant).

Why it matters: Influenza causes annual seasonal epidemics and occasional pandemics (1918, 1957, 1968, 2009) with significant morbidity and mortality, especially in the elderly, young children, pregnant women, and immunocompromised. Secondary bacterial pneumonia (especially S. aureus and S. pneumoniae) is a major complication.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Key surface glycoproteins: Hemagglutinin (HA): binds sialic acid on respiratory epithelium (viral entry); target of neutralizing antibodies. Neuraminidase (NA): cleaves sialic acid to release new virions from cells (viral exit); target of antivirals (oseltamivir, zanamivir).

Antigenic variation: (1) Antigenic drift: minor point mutations in HA/NA genes → gradual changes → seasonal epidemics, requires annual vaccine reformulation. (2) Antigenic shift: reassortment of genome segments between different influenza A strains (e.g., human + avian strains co-infecting a pig — "mixing vessel") → major new subtype → pandemics. Shift only occurs in type A (because of animal reservoirs).

Segmented genome: 8 segments = ability to reassort. This is the molecular basis of antigenic shift.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Antigenic drift vs shift, segmented genome and reassortment, HA and NA functions, pandemic vs epidemic, secondary bacterial pneumonia (S. aureus).

Buzzwords: "Segmented RNA," "antigenic drift," "antigenic shift," "reassortment," "hemagglutinin," "neuraminidase."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Enveloped, (−) ssRNA, SEGMENTED (8 segments), Orthomyxoviridae
  • Hemagglutinin (HA): attachment/entry; Neuraminidase (NA): release/exit
  • Antigenic DRIFT: point mutations → minor changes → seasonal epidemics → annual vaccine
  • Antigenic SHIFT: reassortment of segments between strains → major new subtype → PANDEMICS
  • Shift only in influenza A (requires animal reservoir for reassortment)
  • Pig = "mixing vessel" for human + avian influenza reassortment
  • Complication: secondary bacterial pneumonia (S. aureus, S. pneumoniae)
  • Antivirals: oseltamivir/zanamivir = neuraminidase inhibitors
5 · Active Recall Prompts
  • What are the two major surface glycoproteins of influenza and what does each do?
  • What is the difference between antigenic drift and antigenic shift?
  • Why does antigenic shift only occur in influenza A?
  • What is the molecular basis for reassortment in influenza?
  • Why is the pig considered a "mixing vessel"?

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

Drift = Decades of small changes (minor mutations, epidemics). Shift = Sudden, Segment reassortment (major change, pandemics). Only Type A does both — because it has animal reservoirs (birds, pigs) for reassortment. Type B only drifts (no animal reservoir). The segmented genome is everything: 8 segments can mix and match when two different strains co-infect the same cell.

Viruses — RNA Viruses / Enveloped
HIV
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) is an enveloped, single-stranded positive-sense RNA virus that belongs to the Retroviridae family (genus Lentivirus). It carries reverse transcriptase, which converts viral RNA into DNA (proviral DNA) that integrates into the host genome. HIV-1 is the most common worldwide; HIV-2 is less virulent, mainly in West Africa.

Why it matters: HIV causes acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS), characterized by progressive destruction of CD4+ T helper cells, leading to profound immunodeficiency and susceptibility to opportunistic infections and cancers. Without treatment, AIDS is fatal. ART (antiretroviral therapy) has transformed HIV into a manageable chronic disease.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Structure and entry: gp120 binds to CD4 receptor on T helper cells → conformational change exposes gp41 → gp41 mediates membrane fusion. Co-receptors required: CCR5 (macrophage-tropic strains, early infection) or CXCR4 (T-cell tropic strains, late infection). Individuals homozygous for CCR5-Δ32 deletion are resistant to HIV infection.

Replication: Reverse transcriptase (RNA → DNA) → integrase (inserts proviral DNA into host genome) → protease (cleaves polyproteins into functional viral proteins). All three are drug targets.

Viral load and CD4 count: Viral load measures disease activity/treatment response. CD4 count measures immune status. AIDS defined as CD4 <200 cells/μL or presence of AIDS-defining illness.

Diagnosis: Screening: 4th-gen ELISA (detects p24 antigen + anti-HIV antibodies). Confirmatory: HIV-1/HIV-2 differentiation assay. Viral load by RT-PCR for monitoring.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: gp120/gp41 and entry mechanism, reverse transcriptase, CD4 cell destruction, CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptors, opportunistic infections at different CD4 thresholds, p24 antigen.

Buzzwords: "Retrovirus," "reverse transcriptase," "gp120 binds CD4," "CCR5-Δ32," "proviral DNA," "CD4 <200."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Enveloped, (+) ssRNA, diploid genome (2 copies of RNA), Retroviridae (lentivirus)
  • Reverse transcriptase: RNA → DNA; integrase: inserts into host genome; protease: cleaves polyproteins
  • gp120 binds CD4, gp41 mediates fusion
  • Co-receptors: CCR5 (macrophage-tropic, early) and CXCR4 (T-cell tropic, late)
  • CCR5-Δ32 homozygous = resistant to HIV infection
  • Progressive CD4+ T cell destruction → immunodeficiency → opportunistic infections
  • AIDS: CD4 <200/μL or AIDS-defining illness
  • Screening: 4th-gen combo ELISA (p24 Ag + anti-HIV Ab)
5 · Active Recall Prompts
  • What receptor and co-receptors does HIV use to enter cells?
  • What three viral enzymes are essential for HIV replication?
  • What mutation confers resistance to HIV infection?
  • What defines AIDS by CD4 count?
  • What type of genome does HIV carry and how is it unique among RNA viruses?
  • What is the function of gp41?
  • What does the 4th-generation ELISA test detect?

The three enzymes = three drug targets: reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs, NNRTIs), integrase inhibitors, protease inhibitors. CCR5-Δ32 is the "natural immunity" mutation — homozygotes are protected. The diploid genome (2 RNA copies) is unique among viruses and allows genetic recombination during replication.

Fungi
Candida albicans
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Candida albicans is a dimorphic fungus (yeast at 20°C, pseudohyphae and true hyphae at 37°C/in tissue) that is part of the normal flora of the skin, GI tract, and vagina. It is the most common cause of fungal infections in humans, ranging from superficial mucocutaneous candidiasis to life-threatening invasive/systemic candidiasis.

Why it matters: Candida is the #1 cause of fungal infections in hospitalized and immunocompromised patients. Risk factors include immunosuppression (HIV/AIDS, steroids, chemotherapy), diabetes, broad-spectrum antibiotics (kill bacteria → fungal overgrowth), indwelling catheters, and neonates.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Forms of disease: Oral thrush (white plaques on oral mucosa), vulvovaginal candidiasis ("yeast infection"), diaper dermatitis, esophageal candidiasis (AIDS-defining illness, CD4 <100), candidemia/invasive candidiasis (catheter-associated, high mortality).

Lab identification: Forms germ tubes (short hyphal projections from yeast cells) at 37°C — this test distinguishes C. albicans from other Candida species. Grows on Sabouraud dextrose agar. Pseudohyphae visible on tissue biopsy or KOH prep. Chlamydospores on cornmeal agar.

Virulence: Adhesins (bind to host epithelial cells and plastics/catheters), morphologic switching (yeast ↔ hyphae — hyphae are more invasive), biofilm formation on medical devices, secreted aspartyl proteases.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Dimorphic nature, germ tube test, pseudohyphae, oral thrush, esophageal candidiasis as AIDS-defining, risk factors (antibiotics, immunosuppression, catheters).

Buzzwords: "Germ tube positive," "pseudohyphae," "oral thrush," "esophageal candidiasis," "white plaques."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Dimorphic: yeast at 20°C, pseudohyphae/hyphae at 37°C (opposite of endemic dimorphic fungi)
  • Part of normal flora (skin, GI, vagina) — causes disease in immunocompromised
  • Germ tube test: positive → C. albicans (differentiates from other Candida spp)
  • Risk factors: immunosuppression, antibiotics, diabetes, catheters, neonates
  • Oral thrush: white plaques that can be scraped off
  • Esophageal candidiasis = AIDS-defining illness (CD4 <100)
  • Biofilm formation on catheters and prosthetics
  • Grows on Sabouraud dextrose agar

Candida is "normal until it's not" — it's a commensal that becomes pathogenic when host defenses fail. The germ tube test is the fastest way to confirm C. albicans in the lab. Remember: C. albicans switches from yeast to hyphae at body temp (37°C) — this is the OPPOSITE of the endemic dimorphic fungi (Histoplasma, Blastomyces, Coccidioides) which are mold in the environment and yeast at body temp. Molds
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Molds are multicellular fungi composed of tubular filaments called hyphae. Hyphae form a network called a mycelium. Molds reproduce via spores (conidia). They are classified by hyphal morphology: septate (with cross-walls) vs non-septate/pauciseptate (ribbon-like, wide-angle branching). Major pathogenic molds include Aspergillus, Mucor/Rhizopus (Zygomycetes), and dermatophytes.

Why it matters: Mold infections (invasive aspergillosis, mucormycosis) are devastating in immunocompromised patients (neutropenic, transplant recipients, diabetic ketoacidosis). Dermatophyte infections (ringworm, athlete's foot) are extremely common superficial mycoses.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Aspergillus: Septate hyphae with acute-angle (45°) branching (V-shaped). Causes allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA), aspergilloma (fungus ball in pre-existing lung cavity), and invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (in neutropenic patients). Aflatoxins from A. flavus → hepatocellular carcinoma.

Mucor/Rhizopus (Mucormycosis): Non-septate (pauciseptate), wide-angle (90°) branching hyphae. Rhinocerebral mucormycosis in diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) or immunocompromised — starts in sinuses, invades brain through cribriform plate. Angioinvasive → thrombosis and tissue necrosis.

Dermatophytes: Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton. Cause tinea infections (ringworm, athlete's foot, jock itch, onychomycosis). Infect keratinized tissue (skin, hair, nails). Diagnosed by KOH prep (see hyphae), Wood lamp (Microsporum fluoresces green).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Septate (Aspergillus, 45° branching) vs non-septate (Mucor, 90° branching), mucormycosis in DKA, aspergillosis in neutropenic patients, dermatophyte identification.

Buzzwords: "Septate, 45° branching," "non-septate, 90° branching," "rhinocerebral," "DKA," "fungus ball."

  • Mucor/Rhizopus: non-septate, wide-angle (90°) branching, rhinocerebral in DKA

Antibiotics / Mechanisms / Resistance
Beta-lactam antibiotics
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Beta-lactam antibiotics are the largest and most widely used class of antibiotics. They all share a beta-lactam ring in their molecular structure. The class includes penicillins, cephalosporins (1st–5th generation), carbapenems (broadest spectrum), and monobactams (aztreonam). They are bactericidal.

Why it matters: Beta-lactams are the first-line treatment for many bacterial infections. Understanding their mechanism, spectrum, and resistance patterns is essential for clinical practice and is heavily tested.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Mechanism: Beta-lactams bind to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) in the bacterial cell wall, inhibiting transpeptidation (the cross-linking of peptidoglycan chains). This weakens the cell wall → osmotic instability → cell lysis and death. They are bactericidal, most effective against actively dividing cells.

Resistance mechanisms: (1) Beta-lactamase production (most common — enzyme hydrolyzes the beta-lactam ring). (2) Altered PBPs (MRSA has PBP2a encoded by mecA — reduced affinity for beta-lactams). (3) Decreased porin channels (Gram-negatives — less drug entry). (4) Efflux pumps.

Beta-lactamase inhibitors: Clavulanic acid, sulbactam, tazobactam — co-administered with beta-lactams to overcome beta-lactamase-mediated resistance (e.g., amoxicillin-clavulanate, piperacillin-tazobactam).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Mechanism (inhibit transpeptidation/PBPs), resistance (beta-lactamases, altered PBPs/mecA), beta-lactamase inhibitor combinations, cephalosporin generations, carbapenem spectrum.

Buzzwords: "Beta-lactam ring," "PBP," "transpeptidation," "beta-lactamase," "mecA/PBP2a."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • All share a beta-lactam ring; classes: penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems, monobactams
  • Mechanism: bind PBPs → inhibit transpeptidation → block peptidoglycan cross-linking → cell lysis
  • Bactericidal — require actively dividing bacteria
  • Major resistance: beta-lactamase enzymes (hydrolyze the ring), altered PBPs (MRSA/mecA)
  • Beta-lactamase inhibitors: clavulanic acid, sulbactam, tazobactam
  • Carbapenems: broadest spectrum beta-lactams, resistant to most beta-lactamases
  • Cross-reactivity: ~1–2% cross-allergy between penicillins and cephalosporins
  • Aztreonam (monobactam): Gram-negative only, safe in penicillin-allergic patients
5 · Active Recall Prompts
  • What is the molecular target of beta-lactam antibiotics?
  • What cell wall synthesis step do beta-lactams inhibit?
  • What is the most common mechanism of bacterial resistance to beta-lactams?
  • What genetic element confers methicillin resistance in MRSA?
  • Name three beta-lactamase inhibitors.
  • What is the difference in spectrum between penicillins and carbapenems?

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

Beta-lactam ring = the "Achilles heel" — if bacteria can break it (beta-lactamase), the drug is useless. That's why we add inhibitors to protect it. PBPs are the target — think "Penicillin Binds PBPs." MRSA doesn't make a better beta-lactamase; it makes a different PBP (PBP2a) that beta-lactams can't bind. Carbapenems are the "big guns" — broadest spectrum, but CRE (carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae) are the nightmare scenario.

Mechanisms of antibiotics
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Antibiotics work by targeting essential bacterial structures or metabolic pathways that differ from human cells. The five major mechanisms of action are: (1) cell wall synthesis inhibition, (2) protein synthesis inhibition (30S or 50S ribosomal subunit), (3) nucleic acid synthesis inhibition, (4) folate synthesis inhibition, (5) cell membrane disruption.

Why it matters: Understanding the mechanism of each antibiotic class explains its spectrum of activity, bactericidal vs bacteriostatic nature, side effects, and resistance mechanisms. This is one of the most heavily tested topics in pharmacology and microbiology.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Cell wall inhibitors: Beta-lactams (PBPs/transpeptidation), vancomycin (binds D-Ala-D-Ala, blocks transglycosylation), bacitracin (blocks bactoprenol recycling).

30S ribosomal inhibitors (bacteriostatic except aminoglycosides): Aminoglycosides (bactericidal, cause misreading), tetracyclines (block tRNA attachment). Mnemonic: "At 30, you're still buying Aminoglycosides and Tetracyclines."

50S ribosomal inhibitors (bacteriostatic): Chloramphenicol, macrolides (erythromycin/azithromycin), clindamycin, linezolid. Mnemonic: "Buy AT 30, CELL at 50" — Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin/macrolides, Lincomycin/clindamycin, Linezolid.

DNA/RNA inhibitors: Fluoroquinolones (inhibit DNA gyrase/topoisomerase II and IV), rifampin (inhibits DNA-dependent RNA polymerase), metronidazole (forms free radicals that damage DNA).

Folate pathway: Sulfonamides (inhibit dihydropteroate synthase), trimethoprim (inhibit dihydrofolate reductase). Used together (TMP-SMX) for synergistic sequential blockade.

Cell membrane: Daptomycin (depolarizes Gram-positive membrane), polymyxins (disrupt Gram-negative outer membrane).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: 30S vs 50S inhibitors (and which drugs go where), bactericidal vs bacteriostatic, fluoroquinolone target (DNA gyrase), rifampin target (RNA polymerase), TMP-SMX mechanism, vancomycin mechanism.

Buzzwords: "30S subunit," "50S subunit," "DNA gyrase," "RNA polymerase," "transpeptidation," "D-Ala-D-Ala."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Cell wall: beta-lactams (PBPs), vancomycin (D-Ala-D-Ala)
  • 30S: aminoglycosides (bactericidal, misreading), tetracyclines (bacteriostatic)

Antibiotic test
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Antibiotic susceptibility testing (AST) determines which antibiotics are effective against a specific bacterial isolate. The main methods are disk diffusion (Kirby-Bauer), broth microdilution (determines MIC — minimum inhibitory concentration), and automated systems (e.g., Vitek, MicroScan). The result classifies bacteria as susceptible (S), intermediate (I), or resistant (R).

Why it matters: AST is essential for selecting the most appropriate antibiotic — especially given the rising prevalence of multidrug-resistant organisms. Empirical therapy is started based on likely pathogens, then narrowed ("de-escalation") once AST results are available.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Disk diffusion (Kirby-Bauer): Bacterial lawn on Mueller-Hinton agar. Antibiotic-impregnated disks placed on surface. After incubation, zones of inhibition are measured and compared to standardized breakpoints (CLSI or EUCAST guidelines). Larger zone = more susceptible.

Broth microdilution/MIC: Serial dilutions of antibiotic in broth, inoculated with bacteria. The lowest concentration that prevents visible growth = MIC. Lower MIC = more effective antibiotic. MIC is the gold standard for quantifying susceptibility.

E-test: A plastic strip with a gradient of antibiotic concentrations placed on an agar plate. The MIC is read where the ellipse of inhibition intersects the strip.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Zone of inhibition concept, MIC definition, Mueller-Hinton agar, Kirby-Bauer method, S/I/R classification.

Buzzwords: "Zone of inhibition," "MIC," "Mueller-Hinton agar," "Kirby-Bauer," "disk diffusion."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • AST determines S (susceptible), I (intermediate), R (resistant) for each antibiotic
  • Disk diffusion (Kirby-Bauer): zone of inhibition on Mueller-Hinton agar
  • MIC = minimum inhibitory concentration (lowest conc. preventing growth) — gold standard
  • Broth microdilution: serial dilutions, determines MIC
  • E-test: gradient strip, gives MIC on agar
  • Mueller-Hinton agar is the standard medium for AST (non-selective, supports most pathogens)
  • Results guide de-escalation from empirical to targeted therapy
  • CLSI and EUCAST set breakpoint standards

Tetanus vaccine (toxoid)
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: The tetanus vaccine is a toxoid vaccine — it contains inactivated (formalin-treated) tetanus toxin (tetanospasmin) that has lost its toxicity but retains its immunogenicity. It stimulates the immune system to produce antitoxin antibodies without causing disease. Tetanus is caused by Clostridium tetani, a Gram-positive, obligate anaerobic, spore-forming rod found in soil.

Why it matters: Tetanus is a severe, often fatal disease characterized by muscle rigidity and spasms due to tetanospasmin blocking inhibitory neurotransmitters (glycine and GABA) at the spinal cord. Vaccination is the primary means of prevention.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Tetanospasmin mechanism: Toxin travels retrogradely up motor neurons to the spinal cord, where it cleaves SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin/VAMP) at Renshaw cells and inhibitory interneurons → blocks release of glycine and GABA → unopposed excitatory stimulation → sustained muscle contraction (spastic paralysis). NOTE: this is the opposite of botulinum toxin (which blocks ACh release → flaccid paralysis).

Toxoid vaccine: Formaldehyde inactivates the toxin → no longer toxic but still recognized by the immune system → produces neutralizing antitoxin antibodies. Given as part of DTaP (children) or Tdap (adolescents/adults) or Td (boosters every 10 years).

Tetanus immune globulin (TIG): Provides passive immunity (preformed antibodies) for immediate protection in wound management when vaccination status is uncertain.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Toxoid vaccine concept, tetanospasmin mechanism (blocks GABA/glycine → spastic paralysis), comparison with botulinum toxin (flaccid paralysis), SNARE protein cleavage.

Buzzwords: "Toxoid," "spastic paralysis," "blocks glycine/GABA," "Renshaw cells," "SNARE protein."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Toxoid vaccine = inactivated toxin (formaldehyde-treated), not live

BCG vaccine
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: The Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine is a live-attenuated vaccine derived from Mycobacterium bovis. It is one of the most widely administered vaccines worldwide but is NOT part of the routine vaccination schedule in low-prevalence countries like the US.

Why it matters: TB remains a leading infectious killer globally. BCG provides significant protection against disseminated and meningeal TB in children, though its efficacy against pulmonary TB in adults is highly variable (0–80% depending on the study and population).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Contraindications: Immunocompromised patients (it's a live vaccine — risk of disseminated BCG infection), HIV-positive individuals.

Streptococci
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Streptococci are Gram-positive cocci that arrange in chains or pairs. They are catalase-negative, which distinguishes them from Staphylococci. Classified by hemolysis pattern on blood agar (alpha, beta, gamma) and by Lancefield grouping (carbohydrate antigens: A, B, D, etc.).

Why it matters: Streptococci cause pharyngitis, skin infections, pneumonia, meningitis, neonatal sepsis, urinary infections, and post-infectious sequelae like rheumatic fever and glomerulonephritis. Group A Strep (S. pyogenes) and Group B Strep (S. agalactiae) are especially high-yield.

Key characteristics: Catalase-negative, classified by hemolysis: alpha-hemolytic (partial, green zone — S. pneumoniae, viridans group), beta-hemolytic (complete, clear zone — Group A and B), gamma (no hemolysis — Enterococci, Group D).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Group A Strep (S. pyogenes): Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-sensitive, PYR-positive. Causes pharyngitis, impetigo, cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis, scarlet fever. Virulence: M protein (anti-phagocytic, basis for molecular mimicry with cardiac tissue), streptolysin O (ASO titer), hyaluronidase, streptokinase.

Group B Strep (S. agalactiae): Beta-hemolytic, bacitracin-resistant, hippurate hydrolysis positive, CAMP test positive. Major cause of neonatal meningitis and sepsis. Screen pregnant women at 35–37 weeks.

S. pneumoniae: Alpha-hemolytic, lancet-shaped diplococci, optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble, polysaccharide capsule (major virulence factor). Causes pneumonia (rust-colored sputum), meningitis, otitis media, sinusitis.

Viridans group: Alpha-hemolytic, optochin-resistant, bile-insoluble. Normal oral flora. Cause subacute bacterial endocarditis on damaged valves (S. mutans = dental caries).

Most commonly tested: Hemolysis patterns, bacitracin vs optochin sensitivity, M protein and rheumatic fever, Jones criteria, ASO titers, GBS screening in pregnancy, S. pneumoniae capsule, quellung reaction.

Clostridium difficile / perfringens
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Clostridia are Gram-positive, obligate anaerobic, spore-forming rods. C. difficile is the most common cause of healthcare-associated diarrhea, producing toxin A (enterotoxin) and toxin B (cytotoxin). C. perfringens causes gas gangrene (myonecrosis) via alpha-toxin (lecithinase/phospholipase C) and also causes food poisoning.

Why it matters: C. difficile infection (CDI) is a major hospital-acquired infection with high recurrence rates. C. perfringens gas gangrene is a surgical emergency. Both are high-yield for understanding anaerobic infections, toxin-mediated disease, and spore biology.

Key characteristics: Both are obligate anaerobes and form endospores. C. difficile spores are resistant to alcohol-based hand sanitizers (require soap and water). C. perfringens is the fastest-growing Clostridium and produces a double zone of beta-hemolysis on blood agar.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

C. difficile: Antibiotic exposure (especially clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins) disrupts normal gut flora → C. difficile overgrowth → toxin production → pseudomembranous colitis. Toxin A attracts neutrophils (enterotoxin); toxin B destroys cytoskeleton (cytotoxin, more potent). Diagnosis: stool PCR for toxin genes or GDH screen + toxin EIA. Hallmark finding: pseudomembranes on colonoscopy. Complications: toxic megacolon, perforation. Recurrence common (20–30%). FMT is highly effective for recurrent CDI.

C. perfringens: Alpha-toxin (lecithinase) degrades cell membranes → tissue necrosis. Gas gangrene: trauma/wound contamination → rapid tissue destruction with gas in tissues (crepitus on palpation). Also causes a self-limited food poisoning (enterotoxin, watery diarrhea 8–16 hrs after ingestion of reheated meat dishes).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: C. difficile — antibiotic-associated diarrhea, pseudomembranes, toxin A vs B, spores resistant to alcohol sanitizers, FMT. C. perfringens — alpha-toxin/lecithinase, gas gangrene with crepitus, double-zone hemolysis.

Buzzwords: "Post-antibiotic diarrhea," "pseudomembranes," "spore-forming anaerobe," "gas in tissues," "crepitus," "lecithinase."

Differential: Other causes of antibiotic-associated diarrhea, ischemic colitis, IBD flare (for C. diff); necrotizing fasciitis caused by GAS or polymicrobial infections (for gas gangrene).

Pseudomonas aeruginosa
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a Gram-negative, aerobic, non-fermenting rod that is oxidase-positive. It produces characteristic pigments: pyocyanin (blue-green), pyoverdin (yellow-green, fluorescent). It is found ubiquitously in water and soil, and is a major opportunistic pathogen in hospitals.

Why it matters: Pseudomonas is the most common Gram-negative cause of nosocomial pneumonia (especially ventilator-associated), burn wound infections, and is a chronic colonizer of cystic fibrosis lungs. It is inherently resistant to many antibiotics.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

E. coli
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Escherichia coli is a Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic rod and the most common Gram-negative bacterium in the normal intestinal flora. While most strains are harmless commensals, several pathogenic strains (pathotypes) cause significant disease. It is also a lactose fermenter — produces pink colonies on MacConkey agar and shows a green metallic sheen on EMB (eosin-methylene blue) agar.

Why it matters: E. coli is the #1 cause of urinary tract infections (UTIs), a leading cause of neonatal meningitis (K1 capsule), the most common cause of Gram-negative sepsis, a common cause of traveler's diarrhea (ETEC), and a cause of hemolytic uremic syndrome (EHEC O157:H7).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Pathotypes of diarrheagenic E. coli:

  • ETEC (Enterotoxigenic): produces heat-labile (LT) and heat-stable (ST) toxins. LT activates adenylate cyclase (↑cAMP) — similar to cholera toxin. ST activates guanylate cyclase (↑cGMP). Causes watery (traveler's) diarrhea.
  • EHEC (Enterohemorrhagic): O157:H7 serotype. Produces Shiga-like toxin (Stx). Causes bloody diarrhea, can progress to HUS (hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, renal failure). Do NOT treat with antibiotics (increases toxin release).
  • EIEC (Enteroinvasive): invades colonic mucosa, causes dysentery (like Shigella).
  • EPEC (Enteropathogenic): attaching and effacing lesions on intestinal epithelium. Diarrhea in infants.
  • EAEC (Enteroaggregative): "stacked-brick" adherence pattern, persistent diarrhea.

UTI strains: Uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC) use P-fimbriae (pyelonephritis-associated pili) to adhere to uroepithelial cells.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: E. coli pathotypes and their toxin mechanisms, EHEC/HUS association, LT vs ST toxin mechanisms, K1 capsule and neonatal meningitis, green metallic sheen on EMB agar, #1 cause of UTIs.

Differential: Shigella (also causes dysentery, Shiga toxin — but non-motile, non-lactose-fermenting), Salmonella (non-lactose fermenter), Klebsiella (lactose fermenter but heavily encapsulated).

Bacterial spores
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Bacterial endospores are highly resistant, dormant structures formed by certain Gram-positive bacteria (primarily Bacillus and Clostridium genera) in response to adverse environmental conditions (nutrient depletion, desiccation). Spores are NOT reproductive structures — one vegetative cell produces one spore, which germinates back into one vegetative cell when conditions improve.

Why it matters: Spores are among the most resistant biological structures known — they can survive extreme heat, radiation, desiccation, and chemical disinfectants for years to centuries. This makes spore-forming pathogens (C. difficile, C. botulinum, C. perfringens, C. tetani, B. anthracis) extremely difficult to eliminate and clinically significant for infection control and bioterrorism.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Structure: Core (contains DNA, ribosomes, dipicolinic acid + calcium — stabilizes DNA against heat), cortex (thick peptidoglycan), spore coat (keratin-like protein — chemical resistance), exosporium (outermost layer). Dipicolinic acid is unique to spores and is a key factor in heat resistance.

Sporulation: Triggered by nutrient depletion. Vegetative cell → asymmetric division → forespore engulfed → cortex and coat form → mature endospore released.

Germination: Triggered by favorable conditions (nutrients, warmth, water). Spore swells, breaks coat, becomes vegetative cell.

Key spore-forming pathogens: C. difficile (nosocomial diarrhea), C. botulinum (botulism), C. tetani (tetanus), C. perfringens (gas gangrene), B. anthracis (anthrax), B. cereus (food poisoning).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: What organisms form spores, dipicolinic acid, why spores are resistant (heat, chemical), C. difficile spores surviving alcohol hand sanitizers, autoclave conditions to kill spores (121°C, 15 psi, 15–20 min).

Buzzwords: "Dipicolinic acid," "keratin-like coat," "dormant structure," "heat-resistant," "autoclave."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Endospores = dormant, highly resistant structures; NOT for reproduction
  • Formed by Gram-positive bacteria: Bacillus (aerobic) and Clostridium (anaerobic)

Bacteria — Virulence / Genetic Transfer / Resistance
Transformation / Transduction
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Transformation and transduction are two of the three main mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in bacteria (the third being conjugation). Transformation is the uptake of free (naked) DNA from the environment by a competent bacterium. Transduction is the transfer of bacterial DNA from one bacterium to another via a bacteriophage (bacterial virus).

Why it matters: HGT allows bacteria to acquire new genes — including antibiotic resistance, virulence factors, and metabolic capabilities — without vertical inheritance. This drives bacterial evolution and the rapid emergence of resistant and virulent strains.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Transformation: Bacteria must be "competent" (able to take up DNA) — some are naturally competent (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae), others require artificial induction (CaCl₂ treatment, electroporation). Griffith's experiment (1928) demonstrated transformation using S. pneumoniae (rough ↔ smooth). Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty later proved that DNA was the transforming principle.

Transduction — two types: (1) Generalized transduction: any bacterial DNA can be accidentally packaged into a phage head during lytic cycle → transferred to a new host. (2) Specialized transduction: only genes adjacent to the prophage insertion site are transferred when a lysogenic phage excises imprecisely. Classic example: the gene for diphtheria toxin is carried by a lysogenic phage (corynephage β) → only lysogenized C. diphtheriae strains produce the toxin.

Conjugation (for comparison): Requires direct cell-to-cell contact via sex pilus (F pilus), transfers plasmid or chromosomal DNA. Most efficient method of HGT.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Griffith's experiment, types of transduction, lysogenic conversion (toxin genes carried by phages — diphtheria, cholera, botulinum, Shiga toxin, erythrogenic toxin of Strep pyogenes), differences between transformation/transduction/conjugation.

Buzzwords: "Naked DNA," "competent bacteria," "bacteriophage," "generalized vs specialized transduction," "lysogenic conversion."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Three methods of HGT: transformation, transduction, conjugation
  • Transformation: uptake of free/naked DNA from environment; requires competence
  • Griffith's experiment: heat-killed smooth + live rough S. pneumoniae → smooth (transformation)
  • Transduction: DNA transfer via bacteriophage
  • Generalized transduction: any bacterial gene, lytic cycle, accidental packaging
  • Specialized transduction: only genes near prophage site, imprecise excision, lysogenic cycle
  • Lysogenic conversion: phage genes give bacteria new virulence (diphtheria toxin, cholera toxin, botulinum toxin, Shiga toxin, erythrogenic toxin)
  • Conjugation: direct cell contact via F pilus, most efficient HGT method

Vancomycin / Glycopeptides
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Vancomycin is a glycopeptide antibiotic that inhibits cell wall synthesis in Gram-positive bacteria by binding to the D-Ala-D-Ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors, preventing transglycosylation and transpeptidation. It is a large molecule that cannot penetrate the Gram-negative outer membrane, so it is effective only against Gram-positive organisms.

Why it matters: Vancomycin is the drug of choice for serious MRSA infections and is the last-line defense against many resistant Gram-positive organisms. The emergence of vancomycin-resistant Enterococci (VRE) and rare vancomycin-resistant S. aureus (VRSA) is a critical public health concern.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Mechanism: Binds D-Ala-D-Ala dipeptide on the NAM-pentapeptide of peptidoglycan precursors (on the outside of the cell membrane). This physically blocks both transglycosylation (sugar chain polymerization) and transpeptidation (cross-linking). Different from beta-lactams which only block transpeptidation by binding PBPs.

Resistance (VRE): vanA gene (most common, high-level resistance): modifies the target from D-Ala-D-Ala to D-Ala-D-Lac → vancomycin cannot bind. vanB: also modifies target but only induces resistance in the presence of vancomycin.

Does NOT work on Gram-negatives: Vancomycin is too large to pass through porins of the Gram-negative outer membrane.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Mechanism (D-Ala-D-Ala binding), VRE resistance mechanism (D-Ala-D-Lac), why it's Gram-positive only, use for MRSA, Red Man syndrome (histamine release from rapid infusion — not a true allergy).

Buzzwords: "D-Ala-D-Ala," "D-Ala-D-Lac," "glycopeptide," "Red Man syndrome."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Glycopeptide antibiotic — binds D-Ala-D-Ala on peptidoglycan precursors
  • Blocks both transglycosylation AND transpeptidation
  • Gram-positive only (too large to cross Gram-negative outer membrane)
  • Drug of choice for serious MRSA infections
  • VRE resistance: vanA gene → D-Ala-D-Ala changed to D-Ala-D-Lac → vancomycin can't bind
  • Red Man syndrome: histamine release from rapid infusion (rate-related, not allergy)
  • Nephrotoxic and ototoxic — requires therapeutic drug monitoring (trough levels)
  • Oral vancomycin used for C. difficile (acts locally in GI, not absorbed)

Think of it as changing the lock so the key no longer fits. Red Man syndrome is NOT an allergy — it's a histamine release reaction from infusing too fast. Slow the rate and it resolves. For C. difficile: oral vancomycin stays in the gut (not absorbed) → works locally.

Aminoglycosides
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Aminoglycosides are a class of antibiotics (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin, neomycin) that bind irreversibly to the 30S ribosomal subunit, causing misreading of mRNA and producing faulty proteins. They are bactericidal — one of the few protein synthesis inhibitors that kill bacteria rather than just inhibiting growth.

Why it matters: Aminoglycosides are important for treating serious Gram-negative infections and are often used synergistically with cell wall inhibitors (beta-lactams, vancomycin) for severe infections like endocarditis. However, they have significant toxicities (nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Mechanism: Bind irreversibly to 30S ribosomal subunit → cause misreading of mRNA (misincorporation of amino acids) → defective proteins are incorporated into the cell membrane → membrane integrity compromised → bactericidal effect. This is why they're bactericidal unlike other 30S/50S inhibitors.

Spectrum: Primarily Gram-negative aerobic bacteria. Synergistic with beta-lactams (cell wall damage enhances aminoglycoside penetration).

Toxicities: Nephrotoxicity (reversible), ototoxicity (irreversible — both vestibular and cochlear), neuromuscular blockade (rare). Require dose adjustment by renal function. Therapeutic drug monitoring (peak and trough levels).

Resistance: Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (acetylation, phosphorylation, adenylation — encoded on plasmids), decreased uptake, ribosomal mutations.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: 30S binding, bactericidal (unique among protein synthesis inhibitors), nephro/ototoxicity, synergy with beta-lactams, require O₂ for uptake (ineffective against anaerobes).

Buzzwords: "30S subunit," "bactericidal protein synthesis inhibitor," "nephrotoxicity," "ototoxicity," "synergy."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Bind irreversibly to 30S ribosomal subunit → mRNA misreading → defective proteins
  • Bactericidal (exception among protein synthesis inhibitors)
  • Primarily active against aerobic Gram-negatives
  • NOT effective against anaerobes (require O₂-dependent uptake)
  • Synergistic with beta-lactams (enhanced entry through damaged cell wall)
  • Nephrotoxicity (reversible) and ototoxicity (irreversible) — major toxicities
  • Resistance: modifying enzymes (acetylation, phosphorylation, adenylation)
  • Require therapeutic drug monitoring; dose-adjusted for renal function

Aminoglycosides are "-micin" or "-mycin" (gentamicin, tobramycin, streptomycin). The O₂-dependent uptake is why they don't work on anaerobes — no oxygen, no drug entry. Nephrotoxicity is reversible (kidneys regenerate), ototoxicity is permanent (hair cells don't regenerate). "Once-daily dosing" exploits their concentration-dependent killing and reduces toxicity.

Antibiotic resistance mechanisms
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Antibiotic resistance is the ability of bacteria to survive exposure to antibiotics that would normally kill them or inhibit their growth. Resistance can be intrinsic (natural) or acquired (through mutation or horizontal gene transfer). The four major mechanisms of resistance are: (1) enzymatic drug inactivation, (2) target modification, (3) decreased permeability, (4) efflux pumps.

Why it matters: Antibiotic resistance is one of the greatest threats to global health. Understanding the mechanisms helps guide antibiotic choice, develop new drugs, and implement stewardship strategies to slow resistance emergence.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

1. Enzymatic inactivation: Beta-lactamases (destroy beta-lactam ring), aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (acetylation, phosphorylation, adenylation), chloramphenicol acetyltransferase.

2. Target modification/alteration: PBP2a in MRSA (mecA), D-Ala-D-Lac in VRE (vanA), altered DNA gyrase (fluoroquinolone resistance), methylation of 23S rRNA (macrolide resistance — erm genes).

3. Decreased permeability: Loss/modification of porins in Gram-negative outer membrane → reduced drug entry (e.g., carbapenem resistance in Pseudomonas).

4. Efflux pumps: Actively pump antibiotics out of the cell before they reach target. Multidrug efflux pumps can confer resistance to multiple drug classes simultaneously. Common in Pseudomonas and other Gram-negatives.

Acquisition of resistance: Mutation (vertical), or horizontal gene transfer: transformation, transduction, conjugation (plasmid-mediated — most clinically significant for rapid spread).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: The four mechanisms with specific examples (especially MRSA/mecA, VRE/vanA, beta-lactamases/ESBLs), plasmid-mediated resistance, CRE, horizontal gene transfer.

Buzzwords: "Beta-lactamase," "mecA," "vanA," "efflux pump," "porin loss," "ESBL," "CRE."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Four mechanisms: enzymatic inactivation, target modification, decreased permeability, efflux pumps
  • Beta-lactamase = most common resistance to beta-lactams; ESBLs hydrolyze 3rd-gen cephalosporins
  • MRSA: mecA → PBP2a (target modification)
  • VRE: vanA → D-Ala-D-Lac (target modification)
  • Porin loss: Gram-negative outer membrane becomes less permeable
  • Efflux pumps: pump drug out of cell, often multidrug (MDR)
  • Resistance genes spread by plasmids (conjugation) — fastest horizontal spread
  • CRE (carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae) = "nightmare bacteria"
5 · Active Recall Prompts

  • What enzyme is the most common cause of beta-lactam resistance?
  • How does MRSA resist beta-lactam antibiotics?
  • How does VRE resist vancomycin?
  • How do efflux pumps confer multidrug resistance?
  • What is an ESBL and what antibiotics does it affect?
  • What is the most important horizontal gene transfer method for spreading resistance?
  • Why are CRE considered "nightmare bacteria"?
6 · Additional Insights / Tips

The four mechanisms can be remembered with the mnemonic: "Destroy, Dodge, Decrease entry, Dump out" — Destroy the drug (enzymes), Dodge the drug (altered target), Decrease entry (porin loss), Dump it out (efflux). Each major resistant organism has a signature: MRSA = altered PBP, VRE = altered target (D-Ala-D-Lac), ESBL = enzyme, CRE = carbapenemase enzyme. Plasmid transfer via conjugation is why resistance spreads so fast in hospitals.

Bacteria — Cultures / Lab Methods
Stool culture
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: A stool culture involves collecting a fecal sample and inoculating it onto selective and differential media to identify enteric pathogens. The goal is to isolate pathogenic bacteria from the vast normal flora of the GI tract.

Why it matters: Enteric infections cause significant morbidity and mortality worldwide. Stool culture identifies the causative organism, guides antibiotic therapy when needed, and is essential for public health surveillance of outbreaks.

Key characteristics: Uses selective media that suppress normal flora while allowing pathogens to grow. Key media include MacConkey agar, XLD (xylose lysine deoxycholate) agar, Hektoen enteric agar, SS (Salmonella-Shigella) agar, TCBS agar (for Vibrio), Campy-BAP (for Campylobacter at 42°C), and sorbitol-MacConkey (for EHEC O157:H7 — does not ferment sorbitol).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Major stool pathogens: Salmonella (non-lactose fermenter, H₂S+), Shigella (non-lactose fermenter, H₂S−, non-motile), Campylobacter (curved Gram-negative rod, grows at 42°C), E. coli O157:H7 (sorbitol-negative on SMAC), Vibrio (TCBS), C. difficile (anaerobic culture or toxin detection, not routine stool culture).

Lactose fermentation on MacConkey: Differentiates normal flora (E. coli = pink, lactose+) from pathogens (Salmonella, Shigella = colorless, lactose−).

Indicators of invasive/inflammatory diarrhea: Fecal WBCs (leukocytes), blood in stool, mucus — suggest Shigella, Salmonella, Campylobacter, EIEC, or C. difficile.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Selective media for specific organisms, lactose vs non-lactose fermenters, sorbitol-MacConkey for O157:H7, TCBS for Vibrio, Campy at 42°C.

Buzzwords: "Non-lactose fermenter," "colorless colonies," "H₂S production," "sorbitol-negative," "42°C incubation."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Selective media suppress normal flora to isolate enteric pathogens
  • MacConkey: lactose fermenters (pink) vs non-fermenters (colorless)
  • Salmonella: colorless on MacConkey, black (H₂S) on XLD/Hektoen
  • Shigella: colorless on MacConkey, NO H₂S, non-motile
  • EHEC O157:H7: does NOT ferment sorbitol → colorless on sorbitol-MacConkey
  • Vibrio: TCBS agar (yellow colonies)
  • Campylobacter: 42°C on Campy-BAP (microaerophilic)
  • C. difficile diagnosed by toxin assay/PCR, not routine stool culture

ELISA / PCR detection methods
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay) and PCR (polymerase chain reaction) are two of the most important laboratory detection methods in microbiology. ELISA detects antigens or antibodies using enzyme-linked reactions. PCR detects and amplifies specific DNA/RNA sequences. Both are highly sensitive and specific.

Why it matters: ELISA is the primary screening test for many infections (HIV, HBV, HCV). PCR is the gold standard for diagnosing many infections with higher sensitivity and specificity than culture (Chlamydia, gonorrhea, TB, COVID-19, viral loads).

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

ELISA types: (1) Direct: antigen coated on plate → enzyme-linked antibody added → substrate → color change. (2) Indirect: antigen coated → patient serum added (antibody binds if present) → enzyme-linked secondary antibody → substrate → color. Most common for Ab detection. (3) Sandwich: capture antibody → antigen (from sample) binds → detection antibody → used for Ag detection (e.g., p24 antigen in HIV). (4) Competitive: measures antigen by competition with labeled antigen.

PCR: Denature dsDNA (95°C) → anneal primers (55–65°C) → extend with DNA polymerase (72°C, Taq polymerase). Each cycle doubles the target DNA → exponential amplification. RT-PCR (reverse transcriptase PCR): converts RNA → cDNA first, then amplifies. Used for RNA viruses (HIV viral load, HCV, SARS-CoV-2). Quantitative/real-time PCR (qPCR): measures amplification in real time → quantifies pathogen load.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: ELISA as screening (high sensitivity), Western blot/confirmatory for HIV, PCR as gold standard for many pathogens, RT-PCR for RNA viruses, difference between ELISA types.

Buzzwords: "Enzyme-linked," "sandwich ELISA," "amplification," "Taq polymerase," "reverse transcriptase PCR."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • ELISA: detects antigens or antibodies via enzyme-linked color reaction
  • Indirect ELISA: detects patient antibodies (screening for HIV, HBV, HCV)
  • Sandwich ELISA: detects antigens (p24 in HIV 4th-gen testing)
  • PCR: amplifies specific DNA sequences exponentially (denature → anneal → extend)
  • RT-PCR: RNA → cDNA → amplification (for RNA viruses)
  • qPCR/real-time PCR: quantifies pathogen load in real time
  • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification test) = gold standard for Chlamydia, gonorrhea
  • PCR is highly sensitive — can detect very small amounts of pathogen DNA/RNA

6 · Additional Insights / Tips

ELISA = screening (high sensitivity, some false positives). Western blot or differentiation assay = confirmatory (high specificity). PCR = "molecular photocopy machine" — it finds a tiny piece of pathogen DNA and makes billions of copies so you can detect it. RT-PCR is for RNA viruses because PCR needs DNA to work, so you first reverse-transcribe the RNA into DNA. The 4th-gen HIV ELISA is a combo (sandwich for p24 antigen + indirect for anti-HIV antibodies) — that's why it detects infection earlier than antibody-only tests.

Viruses — DNA and RNA / Enveloped / Capsid
Hepatitis C virus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is an enveloped, single-stranded positive-sense RNA virus of the Flaviviridae family (genus Hepacivirus). It has high genetic variability (6 major genotypes, many quasispecies) due to an error-prone RNA-dependent RNA polymerase with no proofreading.

Why it matters: HCV is the most common chronic blood-borne infection in many countries and the leading cause of chronic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) requiring liver transplantation. Unlike HBV, there is NO vaccine for HCV. However, direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) can now cure >95% of infections.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Transmission: Primarily blood-borne — IV drug use (#1 risk factor), contaminated blood products (before 1992 screening), needlestick injuries. Less efficient sexual and vertical transmission compared to HBV.

Natural history: Acute infection is usually asymptomatic. ~75–85% develop chronic infection (much higher chronicity rate than HBV in adults). Chronic → cirrhosis (20–30% over 20–30 years) → HCC. HCV is the #1 indication for liver transplantation in many countries.

Diagnosis: Anti-HCV antibody (screening) → if positive, confirm with HCV RNA (PCR/viral load). Anti-HCV does NOT indicate immunity — it indicates exposure. A positive RNA confirms active infection.

No vaccine: High genetic variability (quasispecies, 6 genotypes) and antigenic diversity prevent effective vaccine development.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Flavivirus family (+ssRNA, enveloped), highest chronicity rate, no vaccine, blood-borne transmission, diagnosis (anti-HCV → HCV RNA), cryoglobulinemia association.

Buzzwords: "(+) ssRNA," "Flaviviridae," "no vaccine," "highest chronicity rate," "blood-borne," "cryoglobulinemia."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Enveloped, (+) ssRNA, Flaviviridae — NO vaccine available
  • Highest chronicity rate: ~75–85% become chronically infected
  • Blood-borne: IV drug use #1 risk factor
  • Chronic → cirrhosis → HCC; #1 cause for liver transplant
  • Diagnosis: anti-HCV (screening, does NOT mean immune) → HCV RNA (confirms active infection)
  • High genetic variability (RNA polymerase has no proofreading) → no vaccine, quasispecies
  • DAAs (direct-acting antivirals) cure >95% — revolutionized treatment
  • Associated with mixed cryoglobulinemia (immune complex deposition)

Dengue virus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Dengue virus is an enveloped, single-stranded positive-sense RNA virus of the Flaviviridae family. There are four serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4). It is transmitted by the Aedes aegypti (and Aedes albopictus) mosquito — the same vector as yellow fever, Zika, and chikungunya.

Why it matters: Dengue is the most common arboviral disease globally, with ~100–400 million infections/year. Most infections cause dengue fever ("breakbone fever"), but severe dengue (dengue hemorrhagic fever/dengue shock syndrome) can be life-threatening, especially upon secondary infection with a different serotype.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE): The critical concept. After primary infection with one serotype, antibodies are produced. Upon secondary infection with a DIFFERENT serotype, these non-neutralizing antibodies bind the new virus but can't neutralize it → instead, they enhance viral uptake via Fc receptors on macrophages → increased viral replication → more severe disease (DHF/DSS). This is why secondary heterotypic infections are more dangerous.

Clinical: Dengue fever: high fever, severe headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgias/arthralgias ("breakbone fever"), maculopapular rash, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia. Severe dengue: plasma leakage, hemorrhage, organ impairment, shock.

Diagnosis: NS1 antigen (early, first few days), IgM serology (after day 5), RT-PCR.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: ADE mechanism, four serotypes, Aedes mosquito, Flaviviridae, "breakbone fever," secondary infection → DHF/DSS.

Buzzwords: "Breakbone fever," "antibody-dependent enhancement," "Aedes aegypti," "retro-orbital pain," "thrombocytopenia."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Enveloped, (+) ssRNA, Flaviviridae, 4 serotypes
  • Transmitted by Aedes aegypti mosquito
  • ADE: non-neutralizing antibodies from 1st infection enhance 2nd infection with different serotype
  • Secondary heterotypic infection → increased risk of severe dengue (DHF/DSS)
  • "Breakbone fever": fever, headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia, arthralgia
  • Lab: thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, hemoconcentration (in severe cases)
  • Diagnosis: NS1 antigen (early), IgM (after day 5), RT-PCR
  • No specific antiviral — supportive care; avoid aspirin/NSAIDs (bleeding risk)

Measles / Mumps / Rubella
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Three separate viruses prevented by the MMR vaccine. All are enveloped, single-stranded negative-sense RNA viruses (Paramyxoviridae family for measles and mumps; Togaviridae/Matonaviridae for rubella). Measles (rubeola) is caused by Morbillivirus, mumps by Paramyxovirus/Rubulavirus, and rubella (German measles) by Rubivirus.

Why it matters: Measles is one of the most contagious infections known (R₀ = 12–18). Mumps causes parotitis and orchitis. Rubella in pregnancy causes congenital rubella syndrome (devastating birth defects). Vaccination (MMR — live-attenuated) has dramatically reduced all three.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Measles: Prodrome of cough, coryza, conjunctivitis (the 3 C's) + Koplik spots (white/blue spots on buccal mucosa — pathognomonic) → maculopapular rash starting at head, spreading downward. Complications: pneumonia (#1 cause of death), encephalitis, subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE — rare late CNS complication). Warthin-Finkeldey giant cells (multinucleated). Measles causes transient immunosuppression.

Mumps: Parotitis (bilateral parotid gland swelling). Complications: orchitis (unilateral, can cause infertility), aseptic meningitis, pancreatitis.

Rubella: Mild illness in children/adults (fever, fine pink macular rash, lymphadenopathy — especially posterior auricular/postoccipital). DANGER in pregnancy: congenital rubella syndrome — sensorineural deafness, cataracts, patent ductus arteriosus (PDA), "blueberry muffin" rash (extramedullary hematopoiesis), intellectual disability.

Rotavirus
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Rotavirus is a non-enveloped, double-stranded RNA virus with a segmented genome (11 segments) and a characteristic "wheel-like" appearance on EM (rota = wheel in Latin). It belongs to the Reoviridae family.

Why it matters: Rotavirus is the most common cause of severe dehydrating diarrhea in children under 5 years old worldwide. Before vaccination, it was responsible for ~500,000 deaths/year globally. Vaccination has dramatically reduced hospitalization and mortality.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Key features: Non-enveloped (survives in GI tract — resistant to stomach acid), dsRNA (segmented — can reassort, like influenza), replicates in the cytoplasm (dsRNA viruses don't enter the nucleus). Destroys the mature enterocytes at the tips of intestinal villi → decreased absorptive surface → osmotic diarrhea (NOT toxin-mediated). Also produces NSP4 — a viral enterotoxin that contributes to secretory diarrhea.

Clinical: Affects children 6 months – 2 years primarily. Watery diarrhea, vomiting, fever. No blood or WBCs in stool (non-inflammatory). Can cause severe dehydration → hypovolemic shock if untreated.

Vaccine: Live-attenuated oral vaccines (RotaTeq = pentavalent, Rotarix = monovalent). Given in early infancy. Slight increased risk of intussusception (small risk).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: #1 cause of diarrhea in children, dsRNA, non-enveloped, segmented genome, wheel-shaped on EM, villus destruction, live oral vaccine.

Buzzwords: "Wheel-shaped," "dsRNA," "segmented," "non-enveloped," "infantile diarrhea," "live oral vaccine."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)

Fungi
Fungal infections (Aspergillus, Cryptococcus)
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: Two major opportunistic fungal pathogens. Aspergillus species (A. fumigatus most common) are septate molds that cause infection primarily in immunocompromised patients. Cryptococcus neoformans is an encapsulated yeast that causes meningitis, primarily in AIDS patients.

Why it matters: Invasive aspergillosis and cryptococcal meningitis are life-threatening infections in immunosuppressed patients. Aspergillus is the #1 cause of invasive mold infection in neutropenic patients. Cryptococcus is the #1 cause of fungal meningitis in AIDS patients.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Aspergillus: Septate hyphae with acute-angle (45°, V-shaped) branching. Spectrum: allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA — hypersensitivity in asthmatics/CF, ↑IgE), aspergilloma (fungus ball in pre-existing lung cavity — hemoptysis), invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (neutropenic patients — angioinvasive, can disseminate). Diagnosis: galactomannan antigen, biopsy showing septate hyphae. Aflatoxins (A. flavus) → hepatocellular carcinoma.

Cryptococcus neoformans: Encapsulated yeast (thick polysaccharide capsule = MAJOR virulence factor). Found in soil contaminated with pigeon droppings. Causes meningoencephalitis in AIDS patients (CD4 <100). Diagnosed by India ink stain (halo around yeast), latex agglutination or lateral flow assay for capsular antigen (cryptococcal antigen test — CrAg), CSF shows ↑opening pressure. Capsule inhibits phagocytosis.

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Aspergillus: 45° septate hyphae, ABPA vs aspergilloma vs invasive, neutropenic patients, aflatoxins. Cryptococcus: India ink, capsule, pigeon droppings, AIDS meningitis (CD4 <100), ↑CSF opening pressure.

Buzzwords: "Septate 45° branching," "galactomannan," "fungus ball," "India ink halo," "pigeon droppings," "polysaccharide capsule."

Immune System
Complement system
▸
1 · Primer / Beginner Overview

What it is: The complement system is a cascade of ~30 serum proteins that, when activated, enhance ("complement") the ability of the immune system to clear pathogens. There are three activation pathways — classical, alternative, and lectin — all converging on C3 convertase and ultimately forming the membrane attack complex (MAC, C5b-9) that lyses target cells.

Why it matters: Complement deficiencies lead to specific infection susceptibilities and autoimmune diseases. Understanding the pathways and their regulation is essential for immunology, infectious disease, and rheumatology questions.

2 · Notable / High-Yield Highlights

Three pathways:

  • Classical: Activated by antigen-antibody complexes (IgM or IgG). C1 binds → C4 → C2 → C3 convertase (C4b2a).
  • Alternative: Activated by microbial surfaces directly (no antibody needed). Factor B, Factor D, properdin. C3 convertase = C3bBb.
  • Lectin (MBL): Mannose-binding lectin binds mannose on microbial surfaces → activates C4 and C2 (like classical but without antibody).

All three converge on: C3 → C3a + C3b. C3b is the key opsonin. C5 → C5a + C5b. C5b initiates MAC (C5b-9) assembly.

Functions: C3b = opsonization. C3a, C4a, C5a = anaphylatoxins (mast cell degranulation, inflammation). C5a = most potent chemotactic factor for neutrophils. MAC (C5b-9) = lysis of Gram-negative bacteria (especially Neisseria).

Deficiencies: Early complement (C1–C4): increased susceptibility to autoimmune disease (SLE — impaired immune complex clearance). C3: severe, recurrent pyogenic infections. C5–C9 (MAC): recurrent Neisseria infections (meningococcus, gonococcus). DAF/CD55 deficiency: paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH).

3 · Exam / Step 1 Relevance

Most commonly tested: Three pathways and their activators, C3b as opsonin, C5a as chemotactic factor, MAC and Neisseria susceptibility, early complement deficiency and SLE, anaphylatoxins.

Buzzwords: "C3b opsonization," "C5a chemotaxis," "MAC (C5b-9)," "Neisseria infections," "SLE association."

4 · Key Points to Remember (Written Exam)
  • Three pathways: Classical (Ab-Ag complex), Alternative (microbial surfaces), Lectin (MBL-mannose)
  • All converge on C3 → C3a + C3b
  • C3b = opsonization (coats pathogen for phagocytosis)
  • C3a, C4a, C5a = anaphylatoxins (inflammation, mast cell degranulation)
  • C5a = strongest neutrophil chemotactic factor
  • MAC (C5b-9) = pore in cell membrane → lysis, especially Gram-negatives
  • C5–C9 deficiency → recurrent Neisseria infections
  • C1–C4 deficiency → SLE (impaired immune complex clearance)
