Streptococcus

Four species dominate boards: S. pneumoniae, Viridans group, S. pyogenes (Group A), and S. agalactiae (Group B). All are gram-positive cocci in chains, all catalase-negative. What makes each dangerous is their unique virulence strategies and the populations they target.

01The Strep Framework — How They All Fit
▾ collapse

The One Rule That Unites All Strep

Every Streptococcus species is gram-positive, catalase-negative, and forms chains. Catalase negativity is the first test to separate Strep from Staph (which is catalase-positive). On gram stain: purple cocci in pairs or chains, never clusters.

Hemolysis — Your First Branch Point

α-hemolytic (partial)

Green/brown halo on blood agar. S. pneumoniae and Viridans group. To distinguish: Optochin (pneumo = sensitive) and Bile solubility (pneumo = soluble).

β-hemolytic (complete)

Clear zone — complete RBC lysis. Group A (S. pyogenes) and Group B (S. agalactiae). To distinguish: Bacitracin (GAS = sensitive) and PYR test (GAS = positive).

02S. pneumoniae — The MCC Machine
▾ collapse

Key Identifiers

α-hemolytic, Optochin-sensitive, bile-soluble gram-positive lancet-shaped diplococci. The lancet shape (pointed oval pairs) is classic on boards. Facultative anaerobe.

Capsule Is Everything

The polysaccharide capsule is the central virulence factor — it directly inhibits phagocytosis by preventing C3b deposition. The Neufeld-Quellung reaction causes capsule swelling when exposed to specific antibodies, used for serotyping. The capsule is why asplenic patients are at catastrophic risk — the spleen clears encapsulated bacteria, so without it, these patients have no fallback.

Asplenia board pearl: Sickle cell (functional asplenia), post-splenectomy → extreme risk for encapsulated organisms. S. pneumoniae is #1 tested. These patients need vaccines AND prophylactic antibiotics.

IgA₁ Protease — Mucosal Evasion

Cleaves secretory IgA at mucosal surfaces, allowing nasopharyngeal colonization without clearance. This is the setup for invasive disease.

Drug Resistance (DRSP)

Unlike MRSA (enzyme-mediated or acquired PBP genes), DRSP alters its existing PBPs — a structural change making beta-lactams bind poorly. This is why DRSP pneumonia uses Vancomycin, and why meningitis always gets Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin (covers DRSP plus Neisseria and H. influenzae).

Vaccines

PCV13/15/20: conjugate vaccine → protein carrier triggers T-cell help → IgG response with memory. For infants and elderly. PPSV23: pure polysaccharide → T-cell independent → IgM response, no memory. Broader serogroup coverage.

03Viridans Group — The Mouth-to-Heart Threat
▾ collapse

Who They Are

S. mutans, S. mitis, S. sanguinis — α-hemolytic, Optochin-resistant, bile-resistant. These last two distinguish them from S. pneumoniae (both are α-hemolytic). Normal oral flora, harmless in healthy mouths.

Why They Cause Endocarditis

S. sanguinis produces dextran, allowing adherence to fibrin deposits on damaged or prosthetic heart valves. A dental procedure → transient bacteremia → bacteria seed the valve → subacute bacterial endocarditis (SBE). This is slow-onset (weeks) compared to S. aureus endocarditis (acute).

Board classic: Dental procedure + prosthetic heart valve or prior endocarditis → give Amoxicillin prophylaxis before the procedure. This is the single most-tested Viridans fact.

S. mutans — Dental Disease

Ferments sugar into lactic acid using biofilm (dextran) to stick to enamel → dental caries. Not a systemic threat, but a common board association.

04S. pyogenes (Group A) — The Toxin Factory
▾ collapse

Key Identifiers

β-hemolytic, Bacitracin-sensitive, PYR-positive. PYR positivity is the test that definitively separates Group A from Group B (GBS is PYR-negative). Nasopharynx colonizer.

M Protein — The Master Virulence Factor

M protein inhibits opsonization and phagocytosis. Its most clinically important feature: molecular mimicry. M protein resembles cardiac proteins — after GAS pharyngitis, anti-M protein antibodies cross-react with heart tissue → Acute Rheumatic Fever. This is why we always treat strep throat: not for the throat, but to eliminate the antigen before this cascade happens. ARF only follows pharyngitis, not skin infections.

Toxins

Erythrogenic exotoxins A/B/C: cause the sandpaper rash and strawberry tongue of Scarlet fever. SPE (streptococcal pyrogenic exotoxins): superantigens → streptococcal TSS (higher mortality than staph TSS). Streptolysin O: highly antigenic → host makes Anti-Streptolysin O (ASO) antibodies. Elevated ASO titer confirms a recent GAS infection even after bacteria are gone — key for diagnosing ARF/PSGN retroactively.

Two post-infectious sequelae: ARF = pharyngitis ONLY → M protein mimicry → heart damage. PSGN = pharyngitis OR skin → immune complex deposition in glomerular BM → nephritis. Different trigger sites, different mechanisms, different organs.

Soft Tissue — Know the Distinctions

Erysipelas: superficial dermis, sharply demarcated. Cellulitis: deep dermis, poorly demarcated, indurated. Necrotizing fasciitis: pain out of proportion to exam, crepitus, rapid spread, skin necrosis — surgical emergency.

05S. agalactiae (Group B) — The Neonatal Threat
▾ collapse

Key Identifiers

β-hemolytic, Bacitracin-resistant, PYR-negative, Hippurate-positive, CAMP-positive. The CAMP test shows GBS enhancing S. aureus hemolysis on agar — producing a classic arrowhead pattern. Colonizes the maternal vagina and GI tract.

Why Neonates Are Vulnerable

GBS ascends from vagina → uterus → chorioamnionitis, then transmits vertically during delivery. Neonates have immature immunity and no pre-existing GBS-specific maternal IgG coverage.

Screening: All pregnant women screened at 35–37 weeks with rectal + vaginal swabs. Positive → intrapartum IV Penicillin or Ampicillin during labor. Not oral, not before labor — intrapartum only.

Neonatal Presentations

Meningitis: bulging fontanelle, lethargy, hypotonia, poor feeding, hyper- or hypothermia. Pneumonia: respiratory distress, lethargy, pallor, hypotension — within 24 hours of delivery.

C5a-ase — Complement Evasion

GBS produces C5a-ase, which cleaves C5a — the complement fragment that recruits neutrophils. By destroying this chemotactic signal, GBS prevents neutrophil migration. In neonates with immature innate immunity, this disables the primary bacterial defense.

S. pneumoniae

General

  • Gram+ lancet-shaped diplococci
  • Catalase −, α-hemolytic
  • Optochin sensitive, bile soluble
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Neufeld-Quellung → capsule serotyping

Virulence

  • Polysaccharide capsule → ↓ phagocytosis
  • IgA₁ protease → cleaves mucosal IgA
  • Pili → adherence + ↑ inflammation
  • DRSP: altered PBPs (not enzymatic)

Diseases

  • Pneumonia: MCC adults; lobar, rusty sputum
  • Otitis media: MCC children
  • Meningitis: MCC adults
  • Sinusitis, pharyngitis
  • Asplenia → encapsulated organism risk ↑↑

Antibiotics + Vaccines

  • PNA PCN-sens → PCN, Amox, Azithro, Levo
  • PNA PCN-resist → Vancomycin
  • Otitis media → Amox-clavulanate
  • Meningitis → Ceftriaxone + Vanco
  • PCV13/15/20 (IgG), PPSV23 (IgM)
Viridans Group

General

  • S. mutans, S. mitis, S. sanguinis
  • Gram+ chains, catalase −, α-hemolytic
  • Optochin resistant, bile resistant
  • Oropharynx colonizers

Virulence

  • Dextran (S. sanguinis) → fibrin adherence on valves
  • Biofilm (S. mutans) → dental caries
  • Pili → mucosal adherence

Diseases + Tx

  • Dental caries/gingivitis (S. mutans)
  • Subacute bacterial endocarditis (S. sanguinis)
  • Dental procedure → bacteremia → valve seeding
  • Prophylaxis (prosthetic valve/prior SBE) → Amoxicillin
S. pyogenes — Group A Strep (GAS)

General

  • Gram+ pairs/chains, catalase −
  • β-hemolytic
  • Bacitracin sensitive
  • PYR positive

Virulence

  • M protein → ↓ opsonization; mimicry → ARF
  • Erythrogenic exotoxins A/B/C → Scarlet fever
  • SPE (superantigen) → Strep TSS
  • Streptolysin O → ASO titer ↑
  • Hyaluronic acid capsule
  • Streptolysin, hyaluronidase

Diseases

  • Tonsillopharyngitis (age 5–15, gray-white exudates)
  • Scarlet fever (sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue)
  • Erysipelas (superficial, sharp borders)
  • Cellulitis (deep, poorly demarcated)
  • Necrotizing fasciitis (pain out of proportion, crepitus)
  • ARF (pharyngitis only) / PSGN (pharyngitis or skin)

Diagnostics + Antibiotics

  • Rapid Strep Test, throat culture
  • ASO titer, anti-DNase B → confirms prior GAS
  • Tx: Penicillin, Ampicillin, Amoxicillin
  • 1st/2nd gen cephalosporins
S. agalactiae — Group B Strep (GBS)

General

  • Gram+ pairs/chains, catalase −
  • β-hemolytic
  • Bacitracin resistant, PYR negative
  • Hippurate positive
  • CAMP positive (arrowhead hemolysis)

Virulence

  • Sialic acid polysaccharide capsule → ↓ phagocytosis
  • C5a-ase → cleaves C5a → ↓ neutrophil recruitment
  • Pilus → mucosal adherence
  • Hemolysins
  • Vaginal/GI colonization → vertical transmission

Neonatal Disease

  • Meningitis: bulging fontanelle, lethargy, hypotonia, poor feeding
  • Pneumonia: respiratory distress, pallor, hypotension within 24h
  • Chorioamnionitis in mother

Screen + Treat

  • Screen at 35–37 weeks (rectal + vaginal swab)
  • Positive → intrapartum IV Ampicillin +/- Gentamicin
  • Also: Penicillin, 1st/2nd cephalosporins, Clindamycin
