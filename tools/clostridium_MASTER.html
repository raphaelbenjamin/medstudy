C. diff · C. perfringens · C. tetani

The Clostridium Genus

Gram-positive, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe bacilli. All catalase-negative — this distinguishes them from Bacillus. Four major pathogens, two distinct disease categories: GI disease (C. diff, C. perfringens) and neurotoxic disease (C. tetani, C. botulinum).

02
C. difficile — Pseudomembranous Colitis
▾ collapse
⭐ Key Concept: C. diff normally colonizes the GI tract peacefully. The problem starts when antibiotics (especially clindamycin or fluoroquinolones) wipe out competing flora — allowing C. diff to overgrow and release toxins.
Toxin A — Enterotoxin

Binds enterocytes → endocytosis → ↑ epithelial permeability → fluid leaks from vessels into lumen → watery diarrhea

Toxin B — Cytotoxin

Inserts pores into cell membrane → inactivates Rho proteins → disrupts actin cytoskeleton → destroys enterocytes → cytopathic

Note: These are named opposite to the classic A/B exotoxin convention — Toxin A is the binding one here, not Toxin B. Classic trick.

Risk Factors High Yield

  • Recent antibiotic use — clindamycin is the classic; also fluoroquinolones, broad-spectrum PCN
  • PPI use (omeprazole) — lower yield but still tested
  • Hospitalization — contaminated hospital equipment, fecal-oral spread
  • Elderly / immunocompromised patients

Clinical Presentation

  • Watery diarrhea (>3 loose stools/day) ± occult blood
  • Abdominal pain, fever, leukocytosis
  • Severe: dehydration → hypovolemic shock → prerenal AKI
  • Pseudomembranous colitis: yellow fibrin exudates on colonic mucosa (colonoscopy)

Toxic Megacolon High Yield

Toxins + massive inflammation + nitric oxide production → colonic dilation (3–4× normal diameter). Leads to: colonic perforation → peritonitis, paralytic ileus (dysmotility without mechanical blockage), shock. Don't do colonoscopy — risk of perforation. Other causes: ulcerative colitis, Crohn's.

Diagnostics

TestSensitivitySpecificityNotes
NAAT (PCR)↑↑↑Best overall test; detects toxin genes
EIA for toxin A/B↓↑↑↑Most specific; needs high toxin quantity
EIA for GDH antigen↑↓Detects all C. diff, not just toxigenic
Colonoscopy——Pseudomembranes visible; avoid in toxic megacolon

Management

  1. Fidaxomicin — first-line for most cases (preferred over vancomycin for recurrence prevention)
  2. Oral vancomycin — used in refractory or severe cases
  3. Metronidazole — last resort only (not first-line)
  4. Fecal microbiota transplant — recurrent C. diff, restores normal flora
03
C. perfringens — Gas Gangrene & Gastroenteritis
▾ collapse

Key Distinguishing Features

  • Non-motile (C. diff is motile)
  • Double zone of hemolysis on blood agar
  • Uses carbohydrates as fuel → releases CO₂
  • α-toxin = phospholipase C (lecithinase)
  • Myonecrosis via cell membrane lysis
  • CO₂ gas production separates fascial planes

Gas Gangrene (Clostridial Myonecrosis) High Yield

↓ Tissue perfusion (trauma, surgery, PVD) → anaerobic environment → C. perfringens proliferates → releases α-toxin (phospholipase C) → lyses cell membranes → myonecrosis + CO₂ gas production

Clinical clues: Pain out of proportion to exam, massive edema, crepitus (gas in soft tissue = crackling sound), red → purple → black skin discoloration, bullae. Gas pockets on imaging ("feathering pattern").

Spontaneous gas gangrene (no trauma): think C. septicum — hematogenous spread from GI tract in patients with colon cancer or IBD. Same association as S. gallolyticus with colon cancer.

Gastroenteritis — Foodborne Illness

Different from S. aureus/B. cereus — C. perfringens involves spore ingestion (not preformed toxin), so bacteria must germinate and release enterotoxin first.

BugMechanismOnsetClassic Food
S. aureusPreformed toxin1–8 hrsPotato salad, custards
B. cereusPreformed toxin1–8 hrsReheated rice
C. perfringensSpore ingestion → toxin12–24 hrsReheated meat

Presentation: watery diarrhea, emesis, abdominal pain. Usually self-limited.

Management — Gas Gangrene

  1. Surgical debridement — remove necrotic tissue urgently
  2. Penicillin + Clindamycin — covers C. perfringens + Group A strep (necrotizing fasciitis coverage)
  3. Hyperbaric oxygen therapy — kills obligate anaerobe by flooding with O₂
04
C. tetani — Tetanus (Spastic Paralysis)
▾ collapse
The Core Concept: C. tetani releases tetanospasmin toxin → travels retrograde via peripheral nerves to CNS → cleaves SNARE proteins (synaptobrevin-2) at inhibitory interneurons → blocks GABA/glycine release → loss of inhibition → spastic (UMN) paralysis. Everything is toxin-mediated, not bacterial invasion.

Morphology

"Drumstick-shaped" — narrow rod with a terminal spherical spore. Found ubiquitously in soil and animal feces.

Pathogenesis Step-by-Step High Yield

  1. Penetrating wound (nail, bite, burn) → spores inoculated into anaerobic tissue
  2. Spores germinate → bacteria release tetanospasmin toxin
  3. Toxin travels retrograde up cholinergic neurons → spinal cord (CNS)
  4. Toxin reaches Renshaw cells (inhibitory interneurons in ventral horn)
  5. Cleaves synaptobrevin-2 (SNARE protein) → blocks vesicle exocytosis
  6. No GABA/glycine released → α-motor neurons fire without inhibition
  7. Result: sustained muscle contraction, spasms, UMN signs

Clinical Features (UMN Signs)

  • Trismus — "lockjaw"
  • Risus sardonicus — sustained facial grimace
  • Opisthotonus — arching of neck/back
  • Muscle rigidity, spasticity
  • Hyperreflexia
  • Laryngospasm → respiratory failure

Neonatal Tetanus

  • Unvaccinated mother → no transplacental IgG for baby
  • Soil on umbilical stump → colonization
  • ↓ Cry, difficulty feeding/latching
  • Trismus, persistently clenched hands
  • Opisthotonus
  • Prevention: Tdap during pregnancy
Key irreversibility fact: Once tetanospasmin binds SNARE proteins → binding is irreversible. TIG (tetanus immunoglobulin) neutralizes free toxin but cannot reverse already-bound toxin. Symptoms in progress will continue until new SNARE proteins are synthesized.

Management

  1. Airway first — laryngospasm risk is immediate threat
  2. Wound cleaning and debridement — remove remaining bacteria/spores
  3. Metronidazole or Penicillin — kills bacteria, stops further toxin production
  4. TIG (Tetanus Immunoglobulin) — passive immunization, neutralizes free circulating toxin
  5. Tetanus toxoid vaccine — active immunization; note: vaccine targets the toxin, not the bacteria

Vaccination Schedule & Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

DTaP: 5 doses at 2, 4, 6 months, 15–18 months, 4–6 years  ·  Tdap booster: 11–12 years, once in pregnancy  ·  Td: every 10 years adults

💊 Clostridioides difficile C. diff · GI pathogen

General Principles

  • Gram+ bacilli, catalase−, spore-forming
  • Obligate anaerobe, motile
  • Normal GI flora — kept in check by microbiome
  • Toxigenic vs non-toxigenic strains
  • Fecal-oral transmission

Toxins

  • Toxin A (Enterotoxin) → ↑ epithelial permeability → watery diarrhea
  • Toxin B (Cytotoxin) → pore formation → Rho inactivation → destroys enterocytes
  • Both bind enterocytes via endocytosis
  • Both contribute to pseudomembranous colitis

Risk Factors

  • Recent antibiotics — clindamycin (most classic)
  • Fluoroquinolones, broad-spectrum penicillins
  • PPI use (omeprazole)
  • Hospitalization, elderly, immunocompromised

Complications

  • Toxic megacolon → colonic perforation
  • Paralytic ileus
  • Hypovolemic shock, prerenal AKI
  • Pseudomembranous colitis (fibrin exudates)
Diagnostics & Management
TestSensitivitySpecificityNote
NAAT (PCR)↑↑↑Best overall — detects toxin genes
EIA (toxin A/B)↓↑↑↑Most specific but low sensitivity
EIA (GDH antigen)↑↓Detects all C. diff, not just toxigenic

Treatment (in order)

  • Fidaxomicin — first-line
  • Oral vancomycin — refractory/severe
  • Metronidazole — last resort
  • Fecal microbiota transplant — recurrent

Don't Forget

  • Stop offending antibiotic if possible
  • No colonoscopy in toxic megacolon
  • Toxin A = enterotoxin (binding) — reversed from classic A/B naming
  • Toxin B = cytotoxin (destroying)
🦠 Clostridium perfringens Gas gangrene · Gastroenteritis

General Principles

  • Gram+ non-motile bacilli (cf. C. diff = motile)
  • Catalase−, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe
  • Double zone hemolysis on blood agar (inner β + outer α)
  • CO₂ produced as metabolic byproduct

Key Toxins

  • α-toxin — phospholipase C (lecithinase) → lyses phospholipid bilayers → myonecrosis
  • Enterotoxin (heat labile) — diarrheal and emetic properties; released after spore germination

Gas Gangrene

  • Risk: trauma, surgery, peripheral vascular disease
  • Pain out of proportion, crepitus, edema
  • Skin: red → purple → black
  • Gas pockets on imaging (feathering pattern)
  • Spontaneous: C. septicum + colon cancer

Gastroenteritis

  • Spore ingestion (not preformed toxin)
  • Onset 12–24 hours (cf. S. aureus 1–8 hrs)
  • Classic food: reheated meat
  • Watery diarrhea, emesis, abdominal pain
  • Self-limited

Management — Gas Gangrene

  • Surgical debridement (emergency)
  • Penicillin + Clindamycin
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (kills anaerobe)

Complications

  • Shock, multi-organ failure
  • ARDS, DIC
  • Colonic perforation (spontaneous)
⚡ Clostridium tetani Tetanus · UMN · Spastic

General Principles

  • "Drumstick-shaped" gram+ bacilli
  • Ubiquitous in soil, animal feces
  • Catalase−, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe
  • Toxin = tetanospasmin (not bacteria)
  • Retrograde transport to CNS

Pathogenesis

  • Wound → spores germinate → tetanospasmin released
  • Retrograde transport → inhibitory interneurons (Renshaw cells)
  • Cleaves synaptobrevin-2 (SNARE protein)
  • Blocks GABA + glycine → ↑↑ α-motor neuron firing
  • Binding is irreversible

Clinical (UMN Signs)

  • Trismus — lockjaw
  • Risus sardonicus — frozen grin
  • Opisthotonus — neck/back arching
  • Spasticity, hyperreflexia
  • Laryngospasm → respiratory failure

Neonatal Tetanus

  • Unvaccinated mom → no IgG transfer
  • Soil on umbilical stump
  • ↓ Cry, won't feed, trismus
  • Persistently clenched fists, opisthotonus
  • Prevention: Tdap in pregnancy

Management

  • Airway first (laryngospasm)
  • Wound debridement
  • Metronidazole/Penicillin (kills bacteria)
  • TIG — passive, neutralizes free toxin
  • Tetanus toxoid — active immunization

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

  • Clean minor wound + <3 doses → toxoid
  • Dirty wound + <3 doses → toxoid + TIG
  • Clean + ≥3 doses → toxoid if >10 yrs
  • Dirty + ≥3 doses → toxoid if >5 yrs
