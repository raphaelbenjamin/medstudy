The Big Picture: Staph Aureus

Before you memorize details, understand the story. This section builds from the ground up — from what bacteria even are, to how S. aureus fits into the microbiology world, and why its specific features matter clinically.

1
What Are Bacteria, and Why Do We Classify Them?
▾ collapse

Bacteria are single-celled prokaryotes — they have no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles. They live everywhere: on your skin, in your gut, in the air. Most are harmless or even helpful. A small subset are pathogens — they cause disease either by invading tissue directly, or by releasing toxins that damage the host.

Why classification matters for your exam

Microbiology boards don't test whether you can name every organism. They test whether you can identify a bug from a clinical scenario and know how to treat it. Classification gives you a mental framework so you're not memorizing random facts — you're building a decision tree.

The Two Big Questions for Any Bacterium
  • Gram stain? → Tells you cell wall structure → narrows antibiotic choice
  • Shape? → Cocci (spherical) vs. Bacilli (rod-shaped) → further narrows the list
The Gram Stain explained simply: Bacteria have cell walls. Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that traps the crystal violet dye → stain purple. Gram-negative bacteria have a thin peptidoglycan layer + an outer membrane that washes the dye away → stain pink/red. This structural difference is why some antibiotics (like beta-lactams) work differently on gram-positives vs. gram-negatives.
2
Where Does Staph Aureus Fit?
▾ collapse

Its place in the bacterial world

S. aureus is a gram-positive coccus — purple on gram stain, spherical shape. It clusters into grape-like bunches (the word staphyle is Greek for "bunch of grapes"). This clusters appearance alone helps distinguish it from Streptococcus, which forms chains instead.

Staphylococcus

Gram+ cocci in clusters. Catalase positive. Coagulase used to distinguish species. Lives on skin and nares.

Streptococcus

Gram+ cocci in chains/pairs. Catalase negative. Hemolysis pattern used to distinguish species. Lives in throat/respiratory tract.

The single fastest way to separate Staph from Strep: Catalase test. Add hydrogen peroxide. If bubbles form → catalase positive → Staph. No bubbles → Strep. The enzyme catalase breaks H₂O₂ into water and oxygen gas (the bubbles).
The 3 Staph species you need to know
  • S. aureus — the dangerous one. Coagulase positive.
  • S. epidermidis — lives on skin, causes catheter infections. Coagulase negative, novobiocin sensitive.
  • S. saprophyticus — causes UTIs in young women. Coagulase negative, novobiocin resistant.
3
How Bacteria Cause Disease: Exotoxins vs. Endotoxins
▾ collapse

Bacteria hurt you in two main ways: by physically invading tissue (like in pneumonia), or by secreting toxins that do the damage even from a distance. Understanding toxin types is essential for understanding S. aureus diseases.

Exotoxins (Gram+ and Gram−)

Actively secreted proteins. Often heat-labile (destroyed by heat), but some are heat-stable. S. aureus only makes exotoxins (it has no LPS). Types I, II, III depending on mechanism.

Endotoxins (Gram− only)

Part of the bacterial cell wall — LPS (lipopolysaccharide). Released when bacteria die. Causes fever, shock. S. aureus is gram-positive → NO endotoxin/LPS.

Exotoxin Types — Simplified
  • Type I (Superantigens): Don't enter cells. Bind outside the normal MHC II / T cell receptor binding groove → non-specific, massive T cell activation → cytokine storm → shock. Examples: TSST-1, Enterotoxin B.
  • Type II (Membrane-disrupting): Damage cell membranes directly. Example: hemolysins.
  • Type III (A-B toxins): Most exotoxins. The "B" (binding) portion attaches to a cell receptor; the "A" (active) portion enters the cell and causes damage.
4
The Immune System's Battle Against Staph
▾ collapse

To understand why S. aureus virulence factors matter, you first need to know how the immune system normally kills bacteria. Then you can appreciate exactly how S. aureus cheats.

Normal bacterial killing: The oxygen radical pathway

Neutrophils and macrophages use NADPH oxidase to convert O₂ → superoxide radicals → H₂O₂ → HOCL radicals via myeloperoxidase (MPO). These radicals destroy bacteria.

How S. aureus defeats it: Catalase

S. aureus makes catalase, which converts H₂O₂ → O₂ + H₂O before it can become HOCL radicals. Result: the killing pathway is neutralized. This is why patients with Chronic Granulomatous Disease (deficient NADPH oxidase) are extremely susceptible to catalase-positive organisms like S. aureus — they can't even get the killing process started.

How S. aureus defeats antibody-mediated immunity: Protein A

Normally, IgG antibodies bind to bacteria via their Fab region, and the Fc region activates complement → C3b deposits on bacteria → opsonization → phagocytosis. Protein A binds the Fc region of IgG — flipping the antibody "upside down." This blocks complement activation and prevents opsonization, so phagocytes can't recognize and engulf the bacteria.

Connect the dots: CGD patients can't make radicals. S. aureus neutralizes H₂O₂ with catalase. S. aureus also blocks complement with Protein A. And its polysaccharide capsule directly inhibits phagocytosis. It has three separate layers of immune evasion.
5
Antibiotics 101: Why Some Work and Some Don't
▾ collapse

Antibiotics that target bacteria's cell wall synthesis are the backbone of gram-positive treatment. S. aureus has developed clever ways to resist them.

How beta-lactam antibiotics work

Bacteria build their cell wall using enzymes called penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs). Beta-lactam antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) contain a beta-lactam ring that irreversibly binds to PBPs, stopping cell wall synthesis → bacteria die from osmotic pressure.

Resistance mechanism 1: Penicillinase (MSSA)

Some S. aureus strains secrete penicillinase — a beta-lactamase enzyme that cleaves the beta-lactam ring of penicillin, inactivating it. Solution: use penicillinase-resistant beta-lactams (oxacillin, nafcillin) which have a bulky side chain protecting the ring.

Resistance mechanism 2: Modified PBPs (MRSA)

MRSA has a gene (mecA) encoding a modified PBP with very low affinity for ALL beta-lactams. No beta-lactam can bind → the whole class is useless. Treatment must switch to a different mechanism entirely: Vancomycin (binds D-Ala-D-Ala on cell wall precursor, bypassing PBPs entirely), Linezolid (inhibits ribosome), or Daptomycin (disrupts membrane).

General Principles

Carriers & Morphology

  • 1/3 general population carry S. aureus (nares)
  • 4/5 healthcare workers are carriers
  • Gram+ cocci in grape-like clusters
  • Golden/yellow colonies on culture
  • Grows on mannitol salt agar (up to 10% NaCl)
  • Beta-hemolytic, novobiocin sensitive
  • Facultative anaerobe

Key Enzyme Tests

  • Catalase+ → all Staph (vs Strep = catalase −)
  • Coagulase+ → S. aureus only
  • Coagulase− + novobiocin sensitive → S. epidermidis
  • Coagulase− + novobiocin resistant → S. saprophyticus
  • S. aureus does NOT grow on mannitol salt agar? False — it DOES; S. epidermidis does NOT
